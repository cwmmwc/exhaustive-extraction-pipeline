The Kiowa Experience with Fee Patents and Land Loss: A Corpus-Wide Synthesis

### I. The Foundation: From Reservation to Allotment

The Kiowa experience with fee patents and land loss cannot be understood apart from the prior dispossession that created the allotment system. The Jerome Agreement of October 6, 1892, negotiated by commissioners David H. Jerome, Alfred M. Wilson, and Warren G. Sayre at Fort Sill, sought to extinguish Kiowa, Comanche, and Apache title to approximately 2,968,893 acres of reservation land in exchange for 160-acre individual allotments and $2,000,000 — roughly $0.85 per acre for surplus lands (Doc 253).

The agreement was obtained through documented coercion and fraud. Secretary of the Interior C.N. Bliss explicitly recommended against ratification in 1899, stating the Indians "did sign the agreement without a correct understanding of its conditions and consequences" (Doc 253). Interpreter Joshua Given misrepresented terms, telling Indians the opening would not occur for four years (Doc 253). The agreement required three-fourths adult male consent under Article 12 of the 1867 Medicine Lodge Treaty, but only 456 signed against 639 eligible males — 23 short of the threshold — while Indian Agent George D. Day falsely certified only 562 eligible males (Doc 229). A counter-memorial signed by 571 adult males opposed the agreement (Doc 237). Commissioner W.A. Jones reported in January 1899 that the reservation contained only 79,340 acres of agricultural land, making 160-acre allotments physically unworkable for subsistence (Doc 229). U.S. Geological Survey hydrographer F.H. Newell characterized the region as a "famine district," estimating 1,000 acres minimum per family for stock farming (Doc 253).

Despite executive branch objections, tribal supermajority opposition, and documented procedural fraud, Congress ratified the agreement on June 6, 1900 (Doc 229). Kiowa chief Lone Wolf's legal challenge culminated in Lone Wolf v. Hitchcock (1903), in which the Supreme Court upheld congressional plenary power to abrogate Indian treaties unilaterally — a devastating precedent (Doc 248). The reservation was opened to white settlement by President McKinley's proclamation of July 8, 1901, with approximately 2,712,503 surplus acres made available to 165,000 homestead seekers through a lottery system (Doc 240).

Allotment work began in spring 1901, with 2,759 Kiowa, Comanche, and Apache Indians receiving 160-acre tracts under the Acts of June 6, 1900, and March 2, 1895 (Doc 195). An additional 517 children were allotted under the Act of June 5, 1906, and 169 more under the Act of June 25, 1910 (receiving only 120 acres each), totaling 4,402 allotments encompassing approximately 641,901 acres valued at roughly $14.6 million (Doc 195). George Hunt, a Kiowa who served as interpreter and chainman for the allotting crew, later testified that trust patents were issued with a 25-year tax-exempt period — a solemn promise that would be systematically violated (Doc 276).

### II. The Machinery of Forced Fee Patenting

#### A. The Legal Framework

The mechanism that devastated Kiowa landholding was the forced fee patent — a patent in fee simple issued without the allottee's application or consent, converting trust-protected land into alienable, taxable property. The legal authority derived from the Burke Act of May 8, 1906 (34 Stat. 182), which authorized the Secretary of the Interior to issue fee patents to allottees deemed "competent and capable of managing his or her affairs" (Doc 272). This authority was dramatically expanded by Commissioner Cato Sells's "Declaration of Policy" of April 17, 1917, which presumed competency for allottees of one-half or more white blood and mandated fee patents for Indian school graduates "without regard to whether the graduate applies for it or not" (Doc 190).

#### B. The Competency Commissions at Kiowa Agency

In spring 1917, a competency commission composed of Julian H. Fleming, O.M. McPherson, and Superintendent C.V. Stinchecum visited the Kiowa reservation (Doc 243). The commission's own chairman, a Special Indian Agent, filed a memorandum on June 26, 1917, raising serious objections. He described the Kiowa jurisdiction Indians as "extremely non-progressive," estimated that no more than 25 percent of adult restricted Indians spoke English, and noted that most were "just emerging from the blanket stage." Chiefs and headmen, particularly Caddo Chief Enoch Hoag, "strongly objected" to competency work and charged the government with bad faith for attempting to force patents before expiration of the trust period (Doc 223).

The commission processed 109 adults but found only 6 competent, and none would sign applications (Doc 273). Of 53 applications forwarded, 26 were signed and 27 unsigned — every single allottee in the unsigned group "declined to sign" (Doc 243). Superintendent Stinchecum himself refused to sign four competency reports involving full-bloods, citing the Jerome Agreement's plain terms guaranteeing 25-year trust periods (Doc 223). Despite this overwhelming opposition, Secretary of the Interior Franklin K. Lane approved the patents on August 3, 1917, dismissing treaty objections via a memorandum from Judge Pollock (Doc 243).

#### C. The Two Waves of Forced Patents

The documents reveal two principal waves of forced fee patent issuance at Kiowa:

**First Wave — August 24, 1917:** Patents issued to allottees deemed competent by the commission, including both those who signed and those who refused. Named recipients include Anna E. Jones Berry (Kiowa allotment No. 236), Jesse Lee Jones (Kiowa No. 32), Otto Wells (Comanche No. 102/120), Neda Birdsong (Comanche No. 2249), and Chewauwau (Kiowa No. 2410) (Doc 460). Caddo allottees received patents on this same date, including Alice Inkanish Cussen (Caddo No. 12), Ella Sturm (Caddo No. 7), Jesse Sturm (Caddo No. 39), Mattie Sturm (Caddo No. 40), Helena Araspar (Caddo No. 943), Tulethe Araspar (Caddo No. 942), Laura Butler (Caddo No. 269), Nancy Parrish (Caddo No. 73), and Maggie Parrish (Caddo No. 74) (Doc 385).

**Second Wave — December 3, 1919:** A much larger wave issued under the Declaration of Policy, targeting approximately 110 Kiowa, Comanche, and Apache allottees (Doc 217). Named recipients include Ahkaumdomah (Kiowa No. 604), Bointy/Jack Bointy (Kiowa No. 961), David Poolaw/Tsomah (Kiowa No. 2695), Emma Belle Wyatt (Kiowa No. 305), Hattie McKenzie (Kiowa No. 415), Jane Goomby (Kiowa No. 662), John Queton (Kiowa No. 2730), Kaulaity (Kiowa No. 1224), Kauntodle (Kiowa No. 660), Keahtiaukau (Kiowa No. 1280), Lee Kodaseet (Kiowa No. 1278), Lillian Marie Goombi (Kiowa No. 661), Lula Wyatt Willis (Kiowa No. 303), Mabel Sautaukoy (Kiowa No. 1255), Nettie Kaulaity (Kiowa No. 2894), Phylis Poolaw (Kiowa No. 2696), Queton (Kiowa No. 2727), Quoecopah (Kiowa No. 964), Saintohoodie (Kiowa No. 658), Taukauemah (Kiowa No. 2416), Teyou (Kiowa No. 659), Thomas Wyatt (Kiowa No. 302), Tofpi (Kiowa No. 259), Togonegatty (Kiowa No. 1277), Toyebo (Kiowa No. 640), Toykoytodle (Kiowa No. 1253), Tsomah (Kiowa No. 2694), Tsoodle (Kiowa No. 1245), and Yeahgope (Kiowa No. 963) (Doc 414).

A third, smaller wave on August 23, 1920 included Quoecopah (Kiowa No. 964) and Lillian Marie Goombi (Kiowa No. 661) (Doc 399).

#### D. Methods of Coercion

The affidavits reveal a remarkably consistent pattern of coercive delivery across dozens of cases:

**Direct intimidation at the agency office:** Jasper Saunkeah, a Kiowa clerk and interpreter at the agency, repeatedly told allottees they had no choice. Kauntodle testified: "we didn't want our patent, but they just give it to us and said we got to take it" (Doc 406). Yeahgope described returning three times before being "persuaded to take it" under protest (Doc 423). Tsoodle was told by Saunkeah that he would owe taxes regardless of whether he accepted (Doc 411). John Queton testified that Saunkeah told allottees "if they refused their patents they would still owe taxes" (Doc 422).

**Home delivery over explicit objection:** Superintendent Stinchecum and other officials personally delivered patents to Indian homes. Laura Butler (Caddo No. 269) was told "the law would compel me to take it" and that officials would "simply leave it if she refused" (Doc 390). Nancy Parrish's family told officials "there was every reason in the world why we shouldn't have the patent and none why we should," but the patent was mailed to them regardless (Doc 391). Tulethe Araspar told the superintendent she "wasn't ready for it" but was informed that all allottees who were not full-blood Indians were required to accept (Doc 392). Margaret Downing testified that her husband John D. Downing explicitly refused, telling officials he could not afford the tax burden, but was told "it was Washington and he would have to take it" (Doc 454).

**Coercion at payment gatherings:** Chewauwau stated the patent was delivered at a payment gathering at Rainy Mountain and that he was "compelled to accept it before receiving his money," effectively conditioning annuity payments on patent acceptance (Doc 462).

**Circumventing guardians:** Andre Martinez, foster father of Rachel Downing and Hattie McKenzie, refused to accept patents on their behalf and confirmed neither girl had applied. Despite his refusal, the Superintendent went directly to the allottees' home and left the patents (Doc 403).

**Deception about the nature of documents:** Alice Inkanish Cussen testified that the Superintendent and other men pressured her to sign an application "without explaining its consequences, telling her other Indians were signing too." She did not understand what she was signing (Doc 385). Mattie Sturm stated the superintendent told allottees the patents were obligatory — "we thought we had to" accept them and were told "there wasn't any way out of it" (Doc 383).

**Targeting returning veterans:** David Poolaw received his forced patent on December 3, 1919, "shortly after his return from military service," and was told "they all got to take them" (Doc 463).

#### E. Internal Government Resistance

Superintendent Stinchecum refused to sign four competency reports and forwarded Indian protests to Washington (Doc 223). His telegram of June 9, 1917, reported approximately six full-bloods and fifteen mixed-bloods protesting arbitrary patent issuance (Doc 183). His detailed report of June 18, 1917, "earnestly recommended withholding patents" from protesting full-bloods to preserve harmony (Doc 183). The Office responded on August 7, 1917, citing the Burke Act as superseding the Jerome Agreement's trust provisions and ordering issuance regardless (Doc 183).

In the case of Harry Perico (Apache allottee No. 68), Perico's own July 1, 1918 inquiry explicitly stated he preferred his land to remain under government supervision while he worked as a printer. Commissioner Sells directed issuance regardless, invoking the Declaration of Policy (Doc 190).

#### B. Predatory Lending and Fraud

**Quoecopah (Kiowa No. 964):** Jasper Saunkeah, the agency clerk who delivered forced patents, immediately had her patent recorded and then deceived her into signing what she believed was a car purchase agreement for a secondhand Buick but was actually a mortgage on 80 acres. This triggered a cascade totaling $6,000 in encumbrances. Quoecopah received no money. She ultimately deeded the land to Crose in settlement of approximately $350 in debts owed by her husband (Doc 399).

**Keahtiaukau (Kiowa No. 1280):** The patent was filed for record by Fred Baker, who "already had a loan arranged against the land" before the patent was even recorded (Doc 425).

### IV. Quantifying the Losses

- ~110 Kiowa, Comanche, and Apache allottees received forced fee patents under the Declaration of Policy (Doc 217)
- Jasper Saunkeah stated in 1934 that "every Indian issued 'patents in fee' by the Department of Indian Affairs lost their land" (Doc 239)
- By 1934, roughly half of ~1,200 allotments had been lost or sold, leaving ~43.5 acres per member (Doc 239)
- 432 patents in fee issued, ~30,037 acres sold for $883,902.44 (Doc 195)
- From 4,402 allotments / 641,901 acres → 496,336 acres by 1934, with 2,801 landless Indians (Doc 263)
- Between 1953–1958, an additional 120,000 acres sold in the Anadarko area (Doc 186)
