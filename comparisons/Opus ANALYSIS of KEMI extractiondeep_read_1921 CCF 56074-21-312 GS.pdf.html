Deep Reading Analysis: Board of Indian Commissioners Reports on Patent-in-Fee Land Loss (1921)

Document Identification and Context

This document is a compilation of field reports gathered by the Board of Indian Commissioners in 1921, consisting of responses from superintendents, agency farmers, field clerks, and at least one Native person (Samuel Charger, Sioux) to a systematic survey about the consequences of issuing patents-in-fee to Indian allottees. The compilation was organized into numbered bulletins (Nos. 138, 140, 141, 142, 143, 144, 145, 147, 150, 152) and transmitted in batches to Commissioner of Indian Affairs Charles H. Burke between approximately April and October 1921. The cover letters are signed by Malcolm McDowell, Secretary of the Board, with George Vaux Jr. serving as Chairman.

The document's date presents an immediate puzzle: the cover letters are dated 1921, but at least one (page 2) reads "May 6, 1931," and several internal references mention 1931 dates. The filename assigns a date of c. 1921, and the bulk of the content—references to the "Declaration of Policy" of 1917, the competency commissions of 1916-1920, and the recent issuance of "more than 17,000" patents since 1917—firmly anchors the primary data collection to 1921. The 1931 dates on some cover letters likely reflect either OCR errors (1921 misread as 1931) or a later re-transmission of the same materials, possibly when the question of extending restrictions for the Five Civilized Tribes (set to expire in 1931) became urgent.

The Document's Purpose and Architecture

This is not a single report but an orchestrated intelligence-gathering operation. The Board sent standardized questionnaires to 35 superintendents, 110 reservation farmers, and 13 field clerks across the Indian Service, asking them to report on the actual consequences of fee patent issuance. The questionnaire (reproduced on pages 100-102) asked pointed questions:

"How many of your Indians have received patents in fee for original allotments, in what years and what were the size of their allotments and character of land?"

"How many sold their lands and how soon after receiving their fee patents and what did they do with the money received; what are they doing today for a living and what is their general condition as compared to what it was before the sale of their lands?"

"If it were put up to you to determine, what policy would you adopt in regard to releasing Indians from Government control; would you 'turn them loose' at once or would you have each case individually examined?"

The Board explicitly framed this as a response to Commissioner Burke's stated intention to develop "a definite policy covering the whole problem of releasing Indian wards from federal control and supervision." McDowell's cover letter of May 3, 1921 reveals the political context: Burke had told the Board he considered the question "of supreme importance." The Board was positioning itself as an independent source of field-level evidence to counterbalance what it clearly perceived as a too-liberal patent policy.

The Central Finding: Fee Patents as a Mechanism of Dispossession

The overwhelming, nearly unanimous finding across dozens of reservations and jurisdictions is that fee patents functioned as the primary legal mechanism for transferring Indian land to white ownership, with devastating consequences for the Indian allottees. The statistical evidence is staggering in its consistency:

Quantitative Evidence by Reservation:

  • Yankton Sioux (South Dakota): Of 613 patentees, "not to exceed 10 who own their entire original allotments and perhaps not to exceed 50 who own any portion of the land." Superintendent Leech: "probably 400 have absolutely nothing to show for it." (p. 107)

  • Shoshone (Wyoming): "About 90 per cent have either sold or mortgaged their lands." (p. 94)

  • Rosebud Sioux (South Dakota): "Approximately six hundred out of a population of 5,500 have received patents-in-fee... about ninety per cent of the Indians receiving patents-in-fee immediately sold their allotments or mortgaged it and sold it when the mortgage became due." (p. 112)

  • Kiowa Agency (Oklahoma): "Of the last sixty patents issued as the result of the Declaration of Policy, the records of the various County Seats show that of this number twenty were promptly mortgaged. Of this twenty only one of them has been released. Sixteen tracts have been sold outright." (p. 38)

  • Turtle Mountain (North Dakota): "Fully 95% sold their land as soon as they could." (p. 114)

  • LaPointe/Bad River (Wisconsin): "I presume 98% of the 373 Indians who have been granted certificates of competency have sold their land." (p. 119)

  • Coeur d'Alene (Idaho): Of 91 patents issued since 1916, "fifty-four are known to have been sold or lost through mortgages." (p. 132)

  • Flathead (Montana): "Approximately 75 per cent of them have disposed of their original allotments." (p. 200)

  • Fort Peck (Montana): "Every Indian in my district who has received a patent-in-fee for his original allotment has failed." (p. 173)

  • Nez Perce (Idaho): "Of the 300 fee patents issued last year about 200 of the owners have either sold or mortgaged their land." (p. 124)

  • Crow Reservation (Montana): Of 140 reported, "eighty-four or sixty per cent had sold all of their land." (p. 68)

  • Cheyenne River (South Dakota): "Out of 57 citizen Indians in the Whitehouse District... 45 have sold their lands." (p. 86)

  • Pine Ridge (South Dakota): "Approximately ten per cent have their lands now, free from debt." (p. 88)

  • Potawatomi (Kansas): "85 per cent of them have either sold their land or have it plastered with a mortgage up to about its value." (p. 215)

  • Seger Agency (Oklahoma): "Each and every Indian who received his patent" disposed of their allotment, "some at ridiculously low prices." (p. 217)

The Mechanics of Dispossession

The document reveals a remarkably consistent pattern across all jurisdictions, constituting what can only be described as a systemic dispossession pipeline:

Stage 1: The Patent as Trigger

The moment a patent-in-fee was issued, it activated a predatory ecosystem. Superintendent Stinchecum at Kiowa Agency described the pattern with unusual precision:

"In about 99 out of 100 cases the patent is never asked for till the applicant has studied up some plan to alienate it. He never keeps it long enough to teach him the first principles of business independence. It is only a way to show off his restlessness at this small restraint." (p. 42)

The Edmister report from Shawnee provides a vivid case study of how this worked in practice. A loan agent approached him in town asking about two specific Indians' patent applications, revealing that the machinery of dispossession was already in motion before patents were even issued:

"In this instance all plans had evidently been made to eventually alienate this land before the application was made. And if the patent is issued it will pass out of Billy's hands in one of the regular ways." (p. 45)

Stage 2: The Sale or Mortgage

Sales typically occurred immediately—"sometimes before the patent is issued" (p. 9). The Yankton superintendent described the typical sequence:

"Immediately upon the receipt of a patent in fee the Indian is beset by some white man or a number of them who wish to purchase his land, sometimes at prices considerably below the adequate, and sell them automobiles or something the Indian has no particular need of." (p. 108)

At Seger Agency, Superintendent Perkins noted that "Indians disposed of their land immediately upon receipt of the patent (contracts to sell existing, no doubt, prior to receipt of the patent) and were so eager to procure money that most any price was accepted." (p. 217)

The mortgage pathway was equally destructive. At Kiowa, of twenty mortgaged tracts, "only one of them has been released" (p. 38). The pattern was: mortgage for a fraction of value, fail to pay interest or taxes, lose land through foreclosure. Superintendent McQuigg at Turtle Mountain described this precisely: "Once an Indian mortgages... the chances are that he will become discouraged, will default in making payments in full, and will finally lose the land." (p. 115)

Stage 3: Dissipation of Proceeds

The automobile emerges as the single most frequently cited vehicle (literally) for the rapid dissipation of land sale proceeds. This is mentioned in virtually every report. The Yankton superintendent's description is representative:

"He and his family are riding around in some car much more expensive than the average white man can afford. He spends the remainder of the proceeds of the land for gasoline and luxuries, and finally finds himself without any further funds. He next trades off the big car for a cheaper one and gets a few dollars to boot, and finally finds himself afoot." (p. 108)

Stage 4: Dependency and Destitution

The final stage was a return to dependency—but now without the land base that had previously provided at least rental income. The reports describe Indians living off relatives' restricted land, children's trust funds, or county charity. Farmer DeVare at Ponca described the cycle:

"A patent-in-fee simple to a Ponca Indian is but an invitation by the Indian Office for the Indian to go on a money-spending spree that lasts until his land sale money is used up." (p. 10)

The Predatory Ecosystem

The document reveals a sophisticated predatory infrastructure surrounding Indian land:

Land grafters and speculators: Field Clerk Youngblood at Holdenville described them as "commonly called grafters" who "are demanding that the Indians be turned loose... in order that the country may develop and taxes be lowered." He noted: "The honest hard-working farmers living among the Indians do not want them turned loose, as they have seen the result of turning a few loose." (p. 140)

Loan companies: At Holdenville, Archerd reported that "purchasers were able to secure a loan from loan companies on the land even in excess of the purchase price paid for it." (p. 60) This suggests a speculative bubble in Indian land, where the land's value to white buyers exceeded what Indians received.

The Five Civilized Tribes fraud: Youngblood's detailed accounting of fraud in Hughes and Seminole Counties provides the most concrete financial evidence in the document. He calculated that in Hughes County alone, 1,200 mixed-blood Indians (75% of 1,600) were defrauded of 70% of their allotment values, totaling \$873,600 in losses. In Seminole County, 567 mixed-bloods lost an estimated \$544,320. Combined: \$1,417,920 in just two counties. (pp. 141-142)

His nine specific cases document the mechanics: - Mollie Johnson (Roll No. 9130): "executed deeds conveying her lands to various parties without receiving anything like a fair consideration" - Caney Arbor (Roll No. 9613): "executed divers and various deeds on his land at the instigation of the grafters; loss \$900.00. This Indian died destitute." - Jemima Harjo: "induced to sign deed to her allotment only receiving \$400.00 when the land was worth \$1,400.00"

Civil authorities' complicity: Multiple reports note that local and state authorities refused to protect Indians or actively facilitated dispossession. The Fort Peck superintendent observed: "The civil authorities in the state in any place where I have ever been very much dislike and in many cases positively refuse to take up administration of civil authority over the Indians." (p. 127)

The Inherited Land Problem

A critical but underappreciated dimension of the fee patent-land loss relationship emerges from the document's discussion of inherited lands. Field Clerk Snead at Idabel identified this as a separate but equally devastating pathway:

"When an Indian dies his heirs have nothing to do but to await the time required by law, then sign on the dotted line, appear in court and receive his money... It is while the Indian is in possession of this money that he is made the victim of every conceivable scheme designated to rob him." (p. 139)

Field Clerk Phillips at Okmulgee noted that inherited land sales required only probate court approval, and "the probate court holds that its functions are only ministerial and not judicial and pays little attention as to whether or not the consideration named in the deed is adequate." (pp. 59-60)

This created a second channel of dispossession operating alongside the fee patent system, one that affected even restricted Indians through the estates of deceased relatives.

The Structural Impossibility of the Allotment System

Several of the most perceptive reports identify structural features of the allotment system itself that made land loss virtually inevitable, regardless of individual competency:

Geographic Dislocation

Superintendent Kitoh's analysis of Standing Rock is the most sophisticated in the collection. He explains that children allotted in 1908-1909 received land 50-70 miles from their parents' homes because the adjacent water-course lands were already taken. These children "have never seen or traversed their original allotments." When they received patents, they naturally sold land they had never used and could never practically farm:

"A very large number of young Indians, who were industrious and could not be classed as spendthrifts, sold these allotments for no other reason than that they had no use for the land." (p. 82)

This is a crucial insight: the allotment system itself created the conditions for land loss by scattering allotments across vast distances, making consolidated farming impossible.

The Inheritance Fragmentation Problem

As allottees died and their lands were divided among heirs, individual holdings became increasingly scattered and economically unviable. Kitoh noted: "We have many cases of Indians who although they may own several allotments by inheritance it would be almost an impossible task by trade or purchase to fence this land in one contiguous area." (pp. 82-83)

The Leasing Trap

Multiple reports identify leasing as a structural disincentive to farming. Superintendent Carr at Yakima noted: "The high rents of the past few years have been no incentive to the Indian to work when he could be assured of a good income through renting his land." (p. 195) Superintendent Lipps at Nez Perce made the broader point:

"On all of these Northwest reservations it is the sweat of the white man that produces the bread to feed the Indians. True the Indian owns the land, but he does not work it. He leases it to white men and lives off his rentals." (p. 123)

This created a paradox: the system designed to make Indians into farmers instead created a class of rentiers who had no practical experience with money management when they suddenly received large lump sums from land sales.

Voices of Dissent and Complexity

The Indian Voice: Samuel Charger

The most remarkable document in the collection is Samuel Charger's letter from La Plant, South Dakota—the only report written by an Indian person (identified as Sioux). His perspective is strikingly different from the paternalistic tone of the government employees:

"My contention is that the Department is going too fast in releasing us Indians from the Federal control... As a rule we Indians are large land owners and some have the best of locations and well improved allotments, once we own lands and have homes, and are in the prime of life to make some progress but today because we are released from Government control we have disposed of our allotments and our homes and some of us are homeless and are in a very sad plight." (pp. 46-47)

Charger explicitly invoked treaty obligations: "I believe in having competent Indians to classify the Indians of this reservation who should work in harmony with the superintendent." He also noted the devastating impact on veterans: "The American Legion says that all ex Indian soldiers should be citizens, and today the majority of our ex-service boys are without lands and are therefore not independent citizens." (p. 46)

His use of "we" and "us" throughout marks this as a fundamentally different kind of testimony—not observation from outside but lived experience of dispossession.

The Missionary's Candor: W. A. Petzoldt

Baptist missionary Petzoldt at Lodge Grass offered perhaps the most philosophically honest assessment:

"I believe that the Indian has a perfect right to be a prodigal son if he so chooses — certainly he should be permitted to learn from bitter experience and not be kept out of the school of 'hard knocks.'" (p. 73)

Yet he immediately qualified this: "The old Indians should be continued as wards of the Government - most of the others could well be turned loose." His observation that some Crows "refused citizenship as they preferred to escape paying taxes and it was nice to have the Indian office help them out when they got in trouble" reveals the rational calculation behind what officials often dismissed as Indian backwardness.

The Paradox of Competency

Several reports identify what Superintendent Lipps at Nez Perce called "the incompetency of the competent and the competency of the incompetent":

"The older people who have received fee patents, most of whom are uneducated, are, as a rule holding onto their lands and many of them are buying other lands... Few of the younger, educated class are showing so much thrift and business sense." (p. 122)

He explained the paradox: "The older people grew up under different conditions. They had to work and save, or starve... The younger generation have been educated in the Government schools where everything was furnished them free of cost. They have never had to exert themselves." (p. 122)

This fundamentally undermined the premise of the competency system, which assumed that education and English fluency were reliable indicators of the ability to manage property.

What the AI Extraction Missed

The AI extraction captured the entities, transactions, and events with reasonable accuracy but missed several crucial dimensions:

1. The Emotional and Moral Register

The document is saturated with moral anguish from field workers who witnessed dispossession firsthand. Farmer Edmister's account of Mack Johnson's death is devastating:

"When I learned of his death, knowing the condition of his family and the circumstances leading to such condition, I thought of an Indian with whom I was once talking... He said further that he felt the agent, who recommended him for a patent, was much more to blame for 'putting him out of business' than he was himself." (p. 78)

This passage reveals that Indians themselves held the government responsible for the consequences of premature patent issuance—a perspective that has legal and moral implications for treaty obligations.

2. The Systemic Critique

Several writers moved beyond individual cases to structural analysis. Edmister at Shawnee offered a remarkably sophisticated critique of private property ideology as applied to Indians:

"Private property, as represented here by the Indian allotments, has always been one of the most stable institutions of civilization... They were originally pure type socialists, in a property way at least, and had been so trained since their existence; Property was in common, and so proper respect for individual property rights, and in a sense any kind of property rights, has always been one of the slowest lessons that they have to learn from civilization." (pp. 39-40)

This is an extraordinary passage—a government farmer in 1921 identifying the fundamental cultural incompatibility between communal land tenure and individual fee simple ownership, and doing so with a sophistication that anticipates later anthropological analysis.

3. The Gender Dimension

The document contains significant but scattered evidence about gendered patterns of dispossession. Superintendent Hutchison at Shoshone noted: "Great caution should be exercised in the granting of patents-in-fee to females, as it has been my observation that no matter how competent a woman appears, even though married to a white man, her property is soon squandered by her husband, desertions are frequent, and the woman is usually left with several small children without means of support." (pp. 96-97)

Multiple individual cases confirm this pattern: Maggie LaDue, Elnora P. Clavier, Margaret Cooper Penrose, Dadie Pappan, and others lost land through husbands' actions. The Kaw Sub-agency data shows numerous women whose land was sold and money spent by husbands who then abandoned them.

4. The Tax Trap

A critical mechanism of dispossession that the AI extraction largely missed is the role of taxation. Once land was patented, it became taxable. Multiple reports describe Indians unable to pay taxes on land they had never farmed, leading to tax sales. Farmer LeBeau at Cheyenne River noted: "The fee patent Indians were in better condition and were law abiding, but after becoming citizens and land owners... the county authorities at once began making assessments on all the Indians on the ceded portions, and valued their lands as farm lands. Hence taxes were high, overlooking the fact that these lands were allotted as grazing lands." (p. 87)

At Holdenville, Archerd reported: "Among the Creeks when the restrictions have been removed from the surplus allotments the land then becomes taxable. And having had no experience with taxes they are left unpaid, penalties accrue and, in a great number of cases, tax deeds are issued on the land." (p. 60)

5. The Rocky Boy Comparison

Superintendent Mossman at Fort Peck offered a devastating natural experiment. The Rocky Boy Indians had been "free" for sixty years—exactly the condition that fee patent advocates claimed would benefit Indians:

"Those Rocky Boy Indians had every opportunity to become property holders and citizens the same as other persons who were free to come and go in the State without restrictions. After sixty years of this freedom they had come to such a point of degradation and poverty that the people of the State petitioned the Government to provide a reservation for these people to take care of them." (p. 126)

This directly contradicted the argument that removing government supervision would lead to Indian self-sufficiency.

The Policy Recommendations: A Consensus for Restraint

Despite their diverse locations and perspectives, the respondents converged on remarkably similar recommendations:

  1. Individual examination over blanket policies: Nearly every respondent rejected blood quantum or educational attainment as reliable measures of competency. Field Clerk Keith at Muskogee: "Surely this is an individual affair and cannot be determined by the degree of Indian blood, as shown on a roll often incorrect." (p. 148)

  2. Retention of homestead land: The most common specific recommendation was to retain 40 acres (sometimes 20, sometimes 160) as an inalienable homestead. Superintendent Stinchecum at Kiowa proposed: "Make every Indian select forty acres of his original allotment as a homestead and by legislative enactment declare that that forty acres could not be sold or otherwise encumbered or patented to the Indian during the lifetime of the allottee." (p. 38)

  3. Supervised sale of surplus and inherited land: Multiple respondents favored allowing sale of inherited and surplus lands through the agency, with proceeds supervised, while protecting the home allotment.

  4. Education as the only long-term solution: Field Clerk Daniel at Tahlequah offered the most eloquent statement: "Education alone can solve his problem and lift him out of himself, for education alone can give to him the higher ideals to wrestle for, the broader outlook, and the bigger ambitions necessary to arouse him to action." (pp. 53-54)

The Document's Evidentiary Value

This document constitutes the most comprehensive field-level assessment of fee patent consequences produced during the allotment era. Its evidentiary value is extraordinary for several reasons:

  1. Scale: 87 responses from across the entire Indian Service, covering reservations in Washington, Idaho, Montana, Wyoming, North Dakota, South Dakota, Wisconsin, Kansas, and Oklahoma.

  2. Specificity: Hundreds of named individuals with detailed accounts of what happened to their land, providing a granular record of dispossession that can be cross-referenced with county land records.

  3. Consistency: The near-unanimity of findings across vastly different geographic, cultural, and economic contexts strongly suggests that the pattern was systemic rather than local.

  4. Timing: Produced at the precise moment when the Declaration of Policy of 1917 was generating its maximum impact, this document captures the dispossession process in real time.

  5. Candor: The questionnaire format and the Board's independent status encouraged unusual frankness from field workers who might otherwise have been reluctant to criticize departmental policy.

What This Document Proves

The document proves, with overwhelming evidence, that:

  1. Fee patents were the primary legal mechanism for transferring Indian land to white ownership during the allotment era, functioning not as instruments of Indian empowerment but as instruments of dispossession.

  2. The rate of land loss following fee patent issuance was catastrophic—ranging from 60% to 98% across different reservations, with most sales occurring within months of patent receipt.

  3. The competency determination process was fundamentally flawed, with blood quantum, education, and even demonstrated business ability proving unreliable predictors of whether an Indian would retain land.

  4. A predatory infrastructure of land speculators, loan companies, and automobile dealers systematically targeted Indian patentees, often with contracts prepared before patents were even issued.

  5. The allotment system itself created structural conditions—geographic dislocation of allotments, inheritance fragmentation, the leasing trap—that made land loss virtually inevitable regardless of individual competency.

  6. Government field workers overwhelmingly recognized the destructive consequences of the fee patent policy and recommended restraint, individual assessment, and homestead protections—recommendations that were largely ignored as the policy accelerated.

Research Leads

  1. County land records for the specific transactions documented here—particularly the Kaw Sub-agency cases with named individuals—could reveal the identities of buyers and the actual prices paid versus appraised values.

  2. The Board of Indian Commissioners' summary and digest that McDowell mentioned preparing (p. 191) would be a crucial companion document showing how this evidence was synthesized and presented to the Commissioner.

  3. Commissioner Burke's response to these reports would reveal whether and how this field-level evidence influenced policy.

  4. The 1931 restriction expiration for the Five Civilized Tribes, referenced by multiple respondents as an approaching crisis, generated its own documentary trail that would show whether the warnings in this document were heeded.

  5. Samuel Charger's other writings and advocacy deserve investigation as an example of Indian resistance to the fee patent policy from within the system.

  6. The Youngblood fraud calculations for Hughes and Seminole Counties could be tested against actual land records to determine whether his estimates were conservative or generous.

  7. The Declaration of Policy of 1917 and its implementation records would show how the "last sixty patents" at Kiowa and similar mass issuances were authorized and executed.

This document stands as one of the most damning internal assessments of federal Indian land policy ever produced—a chorus of voices from within the system itself testifying that the fee patent policy was, in practice, a mechanism for the systematic dispossession of Native peoples from their land.
