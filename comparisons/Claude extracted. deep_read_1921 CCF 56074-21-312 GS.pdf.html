Deep Reading Analysis: 1921 Board of Indian Commissioners Reports on Patent-in-Fee Land Loss

Document Identification and Context

This document is a compilation of field reports gathered by the Board of Indian Commissioners in 1921, consisting of multiple bulletins (Nos. 138, 140, 141, 142, 143, 144, 145, 147, 150, 152) transmitted to Commissioner of Indian Affairs Charles H. Burke between approximately April and October 1921. Secretary Malcolm McDowell coordinated the survey, which solicited responses from 35 superintendents, 110 reservation farmers, 13 field clerks, and 8 farmers in the Five Tribes jurisdiction. Eighty-seven replies were ultimately received from reservations and field clerk districts across Washington, Idaho, Montana, Wyoming, North Dakota, South Dakota, Wisconsin, Kansas, and Oklahoma.

The document's purpose was to gather empirical evidence about the consequences of the fee patent policy—particularly the "Declaration of Policy" of 1917 and subsequent competency commission actions—to inform whether Congress and the Department should continue, modify, or halt the wholesale issuance of patents-in-fee to Indian allottees.

The Kiowa Experience with Fee Patents and Land Loss

The research question asks specifically about the Kiowa experience. While this document does not contain a dedicated Kiowa-specific report comparable to the detailed case-by-case accounts provided for the Kaw, Ponca, Crow, or Nez Perce, the Kiowa experience is addressed through several critical passages that, when read together, reveal a devastating pattern of land loss and a superintendent's damning assessment of the fee patent policy as applied to his jurisdiction.

1. V. Stinchecum's Report from Kiowa Indian Agency, Anadarko, Oklahoma

The most significant testimony regarding the Kiowa experience comes from V. Stinchecum, Superintendent of the Kiowa Indian Agency at Anadarko, Oklahoma, whose report appears in Bulletin No. 145 (May 26, 1921). Stinchecum's report is remarkable for several reasons: its intellectual depth, its frank condemnation of the fee patent system, and its specific statistical evidence of failure.

a. The Superintendent's Confession of Near-Total Failure

Stinchecum opens with an unusual admission—he had deliberately delayed responding to the Board's inquiry:

"You perhaps have wondered why I have ignored your letter of March 21, 1921, concerning the matter of patents-in-fee."

He explains that he did not refer the questionnaire to his district farmers because "none of them have been on the job long enough to have formed any conclusions that could be of any value whatever." This itself is revealing—the rapid turnover of field staff at Kiowa Agency meant institutional memory about fee patent outcomes was thin, and the superintendent himself was the only reliable witness.

Stinchecum then delivers what is arguably the most devastating single passage in the entire compilation regarding any reservation:

"In my experience here, during a period of more than six years, I cannot now recall but one case where the Indian has made a success in the handling of his land and that Indian could have made just exactly the same kind of a success had his land been held under trust."

This is an extraordinary statement. Out of what must have been hundreds of fee patents issued at the Kiowa Agency—covering Kiowa, Comanche, and Apache allottees—the superintendent could identify only one success, and even that success was not attributable to the patent itself.

b. Statistical Evidence: The Last Sixty Patents

Stinchecum provides specific data on the most recent patents issued under the Declaration of Policy:

"As an illustration, taking the last sixty patents issued as the result of the Declaration of Policy, the records of the various County Seats show that of this number twenty were promptly mortgaged. Of this twenty only one of them has been released. Sixteen tracts have been sold outright. Twenty-four are so far clear, but it is only a question of time until a goodly proprotion of these twenty-four tracts will be encumbered."

Breaking this down: - 20 of 60 (33%) immediately mortgaged, with only 1 mortgage released - 16 of 60 (27%) sold outright - 24 of 60 (40%) still clear, but Stinchecum states "authoritatively, based upon my knowledge of the particular Indians involved and their manner of living" that many of these would soon be encumbered

This means that within a relatively short period after the Declaration of Policy patents were issued, 60% of Kiowa-area allotments were already lost or encumbered, and the superintendent predicted the figure would climb significantly higher.

c. The Fundamental Critique: Blood Quantum and Education as False Measures

Stinchecum's most intellectually significant contribution is his rejection of the entire framework used to determine competency:

"Permit me to say that I believe it is the grossest kind of a mistake to issue patents based on a certain blood status, or educational qualifications as the basis of competency."

He then offers a counterintuitive observation that directly challenges the assumptions underlying the Declaration of Policy:

"I have any number of full-blood Indians who cannot speak a word of English whom I would much rather risk with a patent to their land than an even greater number of bright, educated young fellows who have received all the advantages that the Government can give them."

This passage is historically significant because it inverts the prevailing logic of the era. The Declaration of Policy of 1917 and the competency commissions operated on the assumption that education and mixed-blood status correlated with competency. Stinchecum, with six years of direct observation at Kiowa Agency, found the opposite: uneducated full-bloods were often more prudent stewards of their land than educated mixed-bloods.

d. The Inalienable Homestead Proposal

Stinchecum proposes a specific remedy that reveals the depth of land loss he had witnessed:

"If I were going to make a suggestion it would be to the effect that the Government in some kind of way make every Indian select forty acres of his original allotment as a homestead and by legislative enactment declare that that forty acres could not be sold or otherwise encumbered or patented to the Indian during the lifetime of the allottee."

He notes that Kiowa-area Indians were allotted 160 acres each, and his proposal would protect one-quarter of each allotment permanently. He even addresses the tax implications:

"As an offset to the loss of taxation to the State by reason of such land held under trust, I would make such land taxable at the end of the trust period, but said taxes could not be collected from that property but would be chargeable to any other property, personal or otherwise, that the Indian might own."

This is a sophisticated policy proposal that attempts to balance Indian land protection with state tax interests—suggesting Stinchecum was well aware of the political pressures driving the fee patent policy.

e. The Problem of Extravagance and Imitation

Stinchecum identifies a specific behavioral pattern among Kiowa-area Indians that accelerated land loss:

"The great fault with the Indian with whom I am acquainted is that he does not care to crawl before he walks. If he has twenty acres of land under cultivation he feels he must have the same line of heavy, expensive implements that are owned by his white neighbor who might be working ten times as much land."

This observation about premature acquisition of expensive farming equipment—financed through land sales or mortgages—describes a cycle where the fee patent enabled Indians to leverage their land for equipment purchases they could not sustain, leading to foreclosure and total loss.

f. Inherited Land and the Acceleration of Loss

Stinchecum also addresses inherited land, which was a major factor in Kiowa-area land loss:

"I have often thought, too, that in the case of inherited land the restrictions might be removed as soon as the heirs were determined. In other words, allow the Indian to run through with his inherited property as rapidly as he wants and get him down to a permanent standard basis as quickly as possible."

This passage reveals a pragmatic, if harsh, calculation: since inherited land was being lost anyway through various mechanisms, it might be better to let it go quickly while preserving the original allotment homestead. This suggests that by 1921, the loss of inherited Kiowa lands was already so advanced that Stinchecum saw it as irreversible.

2. The Kiowa Agency in Broader Context: W. D. Brauninger's Testimony

A second witness with direct Kiowa experience is W. D. Brauninger, a farmer who served at multiple Oklahoma agencies:

"In reply to your inquiry of April 2, 1921, you are respectfully advised that I have been engaged in the Indian Service in the capacity of district farmer, in Oklahoma since 1913, as follows: three years with the Cantonment Agency, dealing with the Cheyenne and Arapahoes, and three years at the Kiowa Agency, dealing with the Kiowas, Comanches and Apaches, and for the past 13 months with the Union Agency at Muskogee, dealing with the Cherokees, Choctaws and Creeks."

Brauninger's testimony, while not exclusively about Kiowas, draws on his three years of direct experience at the Kiowa Agency. His assessment is blunt:

"When I take a retrospective view of what we have done and see so many of our young men that we have turned loose without a dollar and without a foot of land, it makes me feel sad. It makes me feel sad to know that, in part, I am responsible for his condition."

This is a rare moment of personal moral reckoning by a government employee who acknowledges complicity in the dispossession process. His use of "we" and "I am responsible" suggests that field employees understood they were participating in a system that was stripping Indians of their patrimony.

Brauninger then offers a sweeping condemnation:

"I would without any hesitancy whatever say that the unconditional removal of restrictions from the Indian citizens would result in the wreck and ruin of the most of them of those tribes whose lands and moneys are restricted and under Government control."

He specifically identifies the mechanism of dispossession:

"The unconditional removal of restrictions would invite hordes of land grafters to invade the Indian countries as a most inviting field where they could indulge their practices upon these innocent and ignorant people, and pauperise them in a very short time."

3. J. C. Hart's Contextual Reference to Kiowas

Superintendent J. C. Hart of the Pawnee Agency provides a brief but significant reference to the Kiowas in his overview of his career experience:

"Speaking only of the tribes that have come under my immediate observation, ie., the Sioux of South Dakota, in 1893-4, Kiowas in 1894, Arapaho in 1895, Eastern Cherokee in 1896-7, Puyallup Consolidated, 1898-9, Oneidas of Wisconsin, 1900-1919, and Pawnee combined since July, 1919..."

Hart's experience with the Kiowas dates to 1894—before allotment—giving him a longitudinal perspective. His general conclusions, while not Kiowa-specific, are informed by this early contact:

"It appears that there is a decided disinclination to work more than is necessary for existence, or to save for the future. Also that the value of lands, original allotments and inherited, is not duly appreciated, either for individual homes or as a source of permanent revenue through rental, and that too much reliance is still placed upon the benevolence of the Government."

4. The Competency Commission's Role at Kiowa Agency

Stinchecum's report contains a crucial passage about the institutional dynamics that drove fee patent issuance at Kiowa and elsewhere:

"Superintendents, I believe, generally have the idea that the Department desires that patents be issued as rapidly as possible, consistent, of course, with the welfare of the Indians, but the burden of the responsibility is upon the superintendent and if the outcome is successful all well and good, but if a mistake is made the superintendent is held responsible."

This reveals the bureaucratic pressure that superintendents faced. The Declaration of Policy created an expectation of rapid patent issuance, and superintendents who resisted were implicitly defying departmental policy. Stinchecum's solution was direct:

"I believe that the best thing that could be done by the Department would be an expression by it to the effect that patents-in-fee should ordinarily be discouraged."

He wanted the Department to explicitly reverse the presumption—from encouraging patents to discouraging them—to give superintendents political cover to protect Indian lands.

5. The Seger Agency Report: Adjacent Kiowa-Comanche-Apache Context

Fred E. Perkins, Superintendent of the Seger Indian Agency at Colony, Oklahoma (which administered Cheyenne and Arapaho Indians adjacent to Kiowa territory), provides testimony that illuminates the regional pattern:

"During the Spring of 1917 a competency commission visited this reservation and examined a large number of Indians... the result was exceedingly disastrous for each and every Indian who received his patent."

"Out of this total number... each and every allotment has been disposed of, some at ridiculously low prices... Indians disposed of their land immediately upon receipt of the patent (contracts to sell existing, no doubt, prior to the issuance of the patent) and were so eager to procure money that most any price was accepted."

The parenthetical observation that "contracts to sell existing, no doubt, prior to the issuance of the patent" is devastating—it suggests that land speculators had pre-arranged purchases before patents were even issued, meaning the competency commission process was effectively a conveyor belt for land transfer from Indian to white ownership.

6. The Cantonment Agency Report: Cheyenne and Arapaho Neighbors

Charles T. Coggeshall and McClain Badgly at the Cantonment Agency (also adjacent to Kiowa territory) provide additional context:

Coggeshall states: "I am personally absolutely opposed to issuing fee patents in a wholesale manner."

Badgly, the Lease Clerk, offers perhaps the most vivid description of the cycle affecting Oklahoma Indians including those in the Kiowa area:

"The Indian on returning home from school has a much better education and training than the average white child and if he has had the proper economic training instilled into his being as the opportunities have presented he is just as competent to take up life's battles as his white brother... Under present conditions there are no incentives. He knows that the Government will look after his interests so he takes his ease."

But then:

"Only a very few live on or cultivate a portion of their allotments, preferring to live in camps or villages... It is a very hard matter to get any of these fellows to do any labor if they have any money to their credit in the office."

And critically:

"As to patent-in-fee Indians under this agency there is not a single Indian who has obtained a patent to any of his lands and has retained possession of them any length of time. In fact they have not held them any longer than the time required to affect a sale."

What the AI Extraction Missed

The automated extraction captured the entities, locations, and individual transactions effectively but missed several crucial dimensions:

a. The Moral and Emotional Register

The document contains remarkable expressions of guilt, sadness, and moral conflict from government employees. Brauninger's "it makes me feel sad to know that, in part, I am responsible for his condition" and Stinchecum's careful, almost anguished analysis represent a crisis of conscience within the Indian Service that entity extraction cannot capture.

b. The Systemic Analysis

Multiple respondents identify the same structural problem: the fee patent system created a one-way valve that converted Indian land into cash, which was then rapidly dissipated. The system was not merely failing individual Indians—it was functioning as designed by land speculators who had pre-positioned themselves to acquire Indian land the moment restrictions were lifted. Stinchecum's observation about pre-existing contracts is a smoking gun that the extraction missed.

c. The Paradox of Competency

Several respondents articulate what the Nez Perce superintendent calls "the incompetency of the competent and the competency of the incompetent"—the finding that educated, mixed-blood Indians were often the worst stewards of their land, while uneducated full-bloods were often the most prudent. This paradox fundamentally undermined the intellectual framework of the allotment and fee patent system.

d. The Role of Automobiles

The automobile emerges as a leitmotif throughout the document—virtually every report mentions Indians purchasing expensive automobiles as the first act after receiving land sale proceeds. The Coeur d'Alene superintendent states: "From an economic standpoint, the automobile is nearly as demoralising to the Indian as whiskey." This is not merely anecdotal—it represents a specific mechanism of wealth destruction that the extraction catalogued individually but did not identify as a systemic pattern.

e. The Gender Dimension

Several reports note that women's patents were particularly problematic. The Shoshone superintendent warns: "Great caution should be exercised in the granting of patents-in-fee to females, as it has been my observation that no matter how competent a woman appears, even though married to a white man, her property is soon squandered by her husband, desertions are frequent, and the woman is usually left with several small children without means of support." The extraction captured individual women's cases but missed this gendered pattern of dispossession through marriage.

f. The 1931 Deadline

For the Five Civilized Tribes, multiple respondents express alarm about the approaching 1931 deadline when all restrictions would automatically expire. Edward P. Snead states: "the thought of the approach of 1931, when the period of restriction will be terminated as to the Choctaw Indians, is to me one of the very greatest concerns." Dean H. Shannon warns: "By Act of Congress all citizens of the Cherokee Nation become unrestricted in 1931. This is entirely too soon, and unless the restrictions are extended by Congress, hundreds of Indians will be pauperized."

Silences and Omissions

What is NOT said about the Kiowas:

  1. No individual Kiowa names are listed. Unlike the Kaw, Ponca, Tonkawa, and Otoe reports that provide detailed case-by-case accounts with names, the Kiowa report is entirely statistical and analytical. This may reflect Stinchecum's deliberate choice to protect individual Indians from further scrutiny, or it may reflect the rapid turnover of field staff that left no one with detailed individual knowledge.

  2. No mention of oil and gas leasing. The Kiowa-Comanche-Apache reservation area was becoming significant for oil production by 1921, yet Stinchecum does not discuss how mineral rights interacted with fee patents. This is a significant omission given that oil leasing was a major factor in land dispossession in Oklahoma.

  3. No discussion of the Jerome Agreement or the specific history of Kiowa allotment. The Kiowa, Comanche, and Apache tribes had resisted allotment through the Jerome Agreement of 1892, which was not ratified until 1900 and was implemented over strong tribal opposition (including Lone Wolf v. Hitchcock, 1903). The document treats Kiowa allotments as fait accompli without acknowledging this contested history.

  4. No Kiowa voices. Unlike Samuel Charger's Sioux testimony, no Kiowa individual is quoted or given space to express their perspective on fee patents. The Kiowa experience is mediated entirely through white government employees.

  5. No mention of the Kiowa tribal structure or cultural factors. The document does not discuss how Kiowa social organization, kinship obligations, or cultural values may have interacted with the fee patent system. Hart's general observation about Indian hospitality customs ("the visitor must be fed, regardless of the length of his stay") may apply to Kiowas but is not specifically attributed.

Evidentiary Value

What this document proves:

  • That by 1921, the superintendent directly responsible for Kiowa Indians could identify only one successful fee patent case out of hundreds
  • That 60% of the most recent Kiowa-area patents had already resulted in land loss through sale or mortgage
  • That the superintendent himself believed the fee patent policy was fundamentally flawed and should be reversed
  • That pre-arranged land sales were occurring before patents were even issued
  • That government employees at the field level understood they were participating in a system of dispossession

What it suggests:

  • That the Kiowa experience was consistent with, and possibly worse than, the national pattern of fee patent land loss
  • That the competency commission system was functioning as a mechanism for land transfer rather than Indian advancement
  • That the 1917 Declaration of Policy accelerated land loss at Kiowa Agency
  • That the automobile served as a specific vehicle (literally and figuratively) for converting Indian land wealth into rapidly depreciating consumer goods

Questions it raises:

  • What happened to the Kiowa lands between 1921 and the Indian Reorganization Act of 1934?
  • How did oil and gas development interact with fee patent land loss at Kiowa Agency?
  • Were the "pre-arranged contracts" documented at Seger Agency also occurring at Kiowa Agency?
  • What was the total acreage lost by Kiowa allottees through fee patents?
  • How did the Kiowa tribal government (or its remnants) respond to the fee patent crisis?

Research Leads

  1. Kiowa Agency records at the National Archives (Record Group 75) should contain the specific patent-in-fee records that Stinchecum referenced, including the "last sixty patents" he analyzed.

  2. County seat records in Caddo, Comanche, Kiowa, and Cotton counties, Oklahoma, would contain the mortgage and deed records Stinchecum consulted.

  3. The Declaration of Policy files (1917) should contain the specific lists of Kiowa allottees targeted for fee patents.

  4. Competency Commission records for the Kiowa Agency would reveal who was examined, who was declared competent, and on what basis.

  5. W. D. Brauninger's personnel file might contain additional reports from his three years at Kiowa Agency.

  6. The Board of Indian Commissioners' summary and digest that McDowell mentioned preparing would provide the Board's own analysis of these findings—this document should be sought in the Board's records.

  7. Congressional hearings on extension of restrictions for Oklahoma Indians approaching the 1931 deadline would likely contain additional Kiowa-specific testimony.

  8. Lone Wolf v. Hitchcock (1903) and subsequent Kiowa legal challenges to allotment provide the legal backdrop against which this administrative dispossession occurred.

  9. The Meriam Report (1928) likely drew on this 1921 survey data and may contain updated Kiowa-specific findings.

  10. Oil and gas lease records at Kiowa Agency would reveal whether mineral rights were being separated from surface rights through the fee patent process, creating a secondary mechanism of dispossession.
