# Forced Fee Patent Issuance on the Crow Reservation: A Corpus-Wide Synthesis

## I. THE LEGAL AND ADMINISTRATIVE FRAMEWORK

The Crow Reservation's experience with forced fee patenting operated within a distinctive legal architecture. The Crow Act of June 4, 1920 (41 Stat. 751) allotted the entire reservation—approximately 2,001,736 acres—making it the largest allotted reservation in the nation, with each member receiving roughly 900–1,000 acres [Doc 2, Doc 33]. This act, layered atop the General Allotment Act of 1887 (24 Stat. 388) and the Burke Act of 1906 (34 Stat. 182), created the legal machinery through which trust-protected Crow lands were converted to alienable fee simple title and transferred to non-Indian ownership.

The 1920 Act established several nominally protective mechanisms: Section 2 limited non-Indian purchases to 640 acres of agricultural or 1,280 acres of grazing land, with absolute ceilings of 1,280 and 1,920 acres respectively [Doc 19]; Section 6 reserved all minerals to the tribe for fifty years [Doc 73]; Section 13 required each allottee to designate a 640-acre inalienable homestead for twenty-five years, with sales limited to 320 acres [Doc 274]. A three-person Enrollment and Competency Commission—comprising the superintendent and two tribal representatives—classified every adult Crow as "competent" or "incompetent," a determination that governed whether an allottee received a fee patent or a trust patent [Doc 73, Doc 328].

These protections proved systematically inadequate. The documentary record reveals that forced fee patenting on the Crow Reservation operated through multiple overlapping mechanisms, involved identifiable networks of federal officials, attorneys, and non-Indian purchasers, and produced catastrophic land loss spanning from the early 1900s through at least the 1960s.

## II. PRIMARY MECHANISMS OF FORCED FEE PATENT ISSUANCE

### A. The Competency Commission and Mass Classification (1920–1924)

The first and most sweeping mechanism was the Competency Commission established under the 1920 Crow Act. The commission classified all enrolled adult Crow members, and those deemed "competent" were scheduled for fee patents. A schedule dated April 2, 1923, signed by Special Allotting Agent Charles E. Roblin and Superintendent C.H. Asbury, certified 111 allottees as competent and as having "applied in writing" for fee patents [Doc 764]. On June 7, 1923, the Assistant Secretary approved the schedules, and the General Land Office began issuing patents [Doc 282].

The allotment schedules transmitted to the Secretary of the Interior on April 26, 1923, encompassed 1,768 allotments covering 1,596,942.93 acres, with pages 314–520 specifically listing lands "to be patented in fee to Indians designated 'competent'" [Doc 282]. This was not a case-by-case determination but a mass administrative action affecting hundreds of allottees simultaneously.

The evidence strongly suggests many of these "applications" were coerced or fabricated. The case of Josephine Collins Terrel (Allotment No. 3137) is paradigmatic: fee patent No. 913157 was issued July 26, 1923, for 200 acres, yet the allottee consistently denied ever applying. In an August 17, 1944 affidavit, she swore she never requested a fee patent and did not want one. The patent was never delivered—it remained in agency files—yet Big Horn County sold the land for delinquent taxes in 1937, with the tax deed issued "in the name of Big Horn County c/o M.H. Tschirgi et al." [Doc 763, Doc 764]. Despite decades of protest by her husband Frank Terrel, the BIA refused cancellation, citing the "presumption of regularity in official action" [Doc 763].

A concentrated batch of fee patents numbered in the 913,000 series was issued on July 26, 1923, affecting dozens of allottees simultaneously [Doc 777, Doc 817]. The tabular summary of forced fee patent cases compiled in the Stanton Collection documents over 500 individual cases spanning from 1903 through 1959, with heavy concentrations in the early-to-mid 1920s [Doc 777, Doc 817].

### B. The "Declaration of Policy" Era (1917–1920)

Before the 1920 Act, the Bureau had already begun forced fee patenting under Commissioner Cato Sells' 1917 "Declaration of Policy," which authorized fee patents for Indians of "half blood or less" deemed competent. On April 2, 1919, Superintendent Asbury submitted to the Commissioner a list of Crow Indians "of half blood or less" who had not yet received fee patents, certifying them as "mentally competent, able bodied and twenty one years of age or over" [Doc 752]. The Department approved the recommendation on June 24, 1919, and the General Land Office began processing patents [Doc 752].

The case of Richard Wallace (Allotment No. 460, 320 acres) reveals the coercive nature of this policy with unusual candor. Superintendent Asbury's January 22, 1920 letter describes Wallace as a full-blood Indian and "about the most efficient Indian on the reservation," who sold approximately $11,000 worth of cattle that year. Yet Asbury explicitly called the fee patent recommendation "a sort of punishment for efficiency," acknowledging that the most capable Indians resisted fee patents because "taxes are painfully sure and regular." He confirmed the Bureau's policy to "issue arbitrarily if necessary patents in fee to Indians, even of full blood, who have proven themselves thoroughly competent" [Doc 754]. The Secretary approved the patent on July 16, 1920 [Doc 754].

### C. The Post-1953 "All-or-Nothing" Policy

After Congress passed Public Law 49 (67 Stat. 42, June 4, 1953), which repealed the homestead restriction under Section 13 of the 1920 Act, the BIA adopted an "all-or-nothing" policy requiring any Crow allottee applying for a fee patent on any portion of their holdings to accept patents on all solely-owned trust lands simultaneously [Doc 669, Doc 795]. Commissioner Glenn L. Emmons' June 13, 1955 letter to the Billings Area Director articulated this policy as designed to stop "piece-meal" patenting, but its practical effect was to force allottees into total removal from trust status [Doc 695].

Robert Yellowtail protested this policy in a March 1954 letter as "the height of foolishness," noting it effectively pushed all Indian trust land onto tax rolls against individual allottees' wishes [Doc 669]. Senator Murray explicitly stated that Congress had refused to enact legislation authorizing forced patenting of competent allottees and that the BIA's policy contradicted congressional intent [Doc 795]. The Crow Agency reported processing approximately 200 fee patent applications following the 1953 repeal [Doc 708].

### D. Individual Congressional Bills as Dispossession Vehicles

When administrative mechanisms proved insufficient—particularly where the 320-acre homestead sale limitation applied—individual congressional bills served as the vehicle for fee patenting. The documentary record contains transcripts of at least twenty separate House and Senate hearings on private fee patent bills for individual Crow allottees between 1946 and 1952, including bills for Richard S. Fisher (970.19 acres) [Doc 300], the Doyle family (4,953.43 acres across five bills) [Doc 341], Joseph J. Pickett (1,200 acres) [Doc 256, Doc 322, Doc 330, Doc 331], Claude E. Milliken (640 acres) [Doc 303], Mark and Estella Wolfe (1,480 acres combined) [Doc 312], and many others.

These hearings reveal a routinized process: Representative Wesley D'Ewart of Montana introduced virtually all the bills; the subcommittee typically acted in minutes with no testimony from the allottee or tribal representatives; and the committee voted unanimously for favorable reporting [Doc 296, Doc 309, Doc 320]. During the 82nd Congress alone, 75 individual bills were introduced to authorize specific Crow land sales or fee patents [Doc 274, Doc 286]. Acting Commissioner William Zimmerman testified in 1949 that since January 1948, 65 patents had been issued on the Crow Reservation alone, and nationally in 1948, 501 patents in fee plus 170 restriction removals affected approximately 106,000 acres [Doc 312].

### E. Pre-Arranged Sales and Predatory Purchasing

The most insidious mechanism combined fee patenting with pre-arranged sales to non-Indian purchasers. The Dillon v. Antler Land Company litigation (Civil No. 891) provides the most thoroughly documented example. Phoebe Wilson Dillon, Crow allottee No. 3636, held 1,040 acres under trust patents issued in 1923 and 1924. Matt Tschirgi, president of Antler Land Company, induced her to apply for a fee patent by advancing $85 in 1949 and ultimately $3,029.06 before the patent issued. On May 21, 1955—while the land was still in trust—she contracted to sell to Antler for $7,280 ($7.00/acre) when the BIA appraised value was $10,400 ($10.00/acre). Tschirgi coached her to conceal his involvement from the agency. The fee patent issued September 19, 1955; she signed a warranty deed October 6, 1955, receiving only $3,711 in cash after deductions [Doc 257, Doc 264, Doc 293].

This was not an isolated case. Superintendent Clyde Hobbs noted that "fee-patented lands were routinely sold to non-Indians through pre-arranged agreements before patents issued" [Doc 8]. The BIA investigation of A.G. Ballensky of Hardin revealed a pattern of unauthorized loans to Crow members—including John Takes Gun, Marie Bull Chief, Louis Reed, James Brown, Virginia Little Light, and others—based on their representations that they had obtained or were obtaining patents in fee, with at least one assignment signed under duress or intoxication [Doc 76].

## III. KEY ACTORS

### A. Federal Officials

**Superintendent C.H. Asbury (1918–1931)** administered the initial allotment and competency classification process, submitted the lists of Indians for forced fee patenting, and advocated for repeal of Section 2's acreage limitations to facilitate non-Indian land purchases [Doc 1, Doc 48, Doc 752, Doc 754]. His 1924 letter opposing Indian voting rights reveals his paternalistic worldview [Doc 82].

**Commissioner Charles H. Burke** oversaw the mass fee patenting of the early 1920s and led efforts to repeal Section 2 [Doc 1, Doc 266].

**Commissioner Glenn L. Emmons** suspended Crow land sales in late 1955 upon discovering Section 2 violations but simultaneously implemented the "all-or-nothing" fee patent policy and "liberalized" fee patent procedures in a July 1955 press release [Doc 695, Doc 696].

**Superintendent L.C. Lippert** processed hundreds of fee patent applications and supervised land sales during the critical 1948–1960 period, exercising broad discretionary authority over competency determinations [Doc 667, Doc 818, Doc 819].

**Area Director Paul L. Fickinger** compiled the confidential October 27, 1955 tabulation identifying over two dozen non-Indian landholders exceeding statutory limits, revealing that 60% of land sales over the preceding four years went to large operators already in violation [Doc 413].

### B. Non-Indian Purchasers and Land Companies

**M.H. Tschirgi and the Antler Land Company** represent the most documented case of systematic land accumulation. The confidential 1955 BIA tabulation identified Antler Land Company/M.H. & F.M. Tschirgi as holding 34,195.75 acres [Doc 413]. A separate analysis showed 42,565.91 acres acquired through various mechanisms—5,870.27 acres via pre-1920 fee patents, 18,611.60 acres through post-1920 patents, 1,860 acres at government sales, and 5,368.61 acres through sales by non-competent Indians [Doc 663]. By 1958, Big Horn County assessed 40,021.02 acres to Antler Land Company [Doc 251]. By 1971, the successor entity Little Horn Land and Livestock Company controlled over 120,000 acres in Big Horn County through deeds or leases [Doc 335]. The Tschirgi family's operations were financed in part by an $1,800,000 loan from the Teamsters' Central States Pension Fund [Doc 251].

**Campbell Farming Corporation**, controlled by Thomas D. Campbell, held approximately 14,762.53 acres according to the 1955 BIA tabulation [Doc 413], with other estimates reaching 25,000–45,000 acres [Doc 258, Doc 715]. Campbell's operations began with a wartime farming lease in 1918 covering up to 150,000 acres at 7½–10% of crops, obtained through what Crow witnesses described as patriotic pressure [Doc 27]. Campbell controlled one-third of his operating area outright and leased the remainder [Doc 662].

**Homer Scott and affiliated entities** (Flying V. Cattle Corp., Trans-West Trust Company, Padlock Ranch Corp., P.K. Ranch Corp., Scott Land and Livestock Company) allegedly amassed approximately 67,000 acres, of which at least 30,000 were acquired directly from Indians when Scott already exceeded the 1,920-acre limit [Doc 707, Doc 722]. Even if completely divested, Scott would still control 157,000 acres under perpetual leases [Doc 707].

**Other major violators** identified in the 1955 tabulation include Henman-Kelly (33,303.28 acres), Warren & Sons (22,505.60 acres), and P.K. Ranch (8,862.28 acres) [Doc 413]. A 1977 Native American Rights Fund study identified 56 non-Crow persons collectively holding over 285,000 excess acres [Doc 259, Doc 736].

### C. Attorneys as Intermediaries

**Harold G. Stanton** of Stanton, Wilson & Hovland in Hardin operated as a central intermediary in fee patent transactions. The documentary record shows him simultaneously representing Crow allottees seeking fee patents, holding mortgages on newly patented land, purchasing land from allottees at nominal consideration, and reselling to non-Indian buyers [Doc 819, Doc 821, Doc 822, Doc 823]. He retained Thelma S. Williams in Washington, D.C. at $35/month to conduct daily surveillance of Bureau of Land Management patent records and telegraph him immediately upon issuance of fee patents [Doc 827]. Stanton also held contracts as Crow Tribal attorney while simultaneously representing white ranchers who leased Crow land, a conflict of interest protested by Urban Bear Don't Walk [Doc 720].

**Bert W. Kronmiller** of Hardin served as both Crow Tribal attorney and Big Horn County Attorney, having previously initiated taxation of Crow personal property in the latter capacity [Doc 668]. He represented non-Indian purchasers seeking validation of illegal land acquisitions [Doc 28] while simultaneously serving as tribal counsel.

### D. Crow Resisters

**Robert Yellowtail**, former superintendent (1934–1945) and lifelong tribal advocate, testified extensively before Congress, opposed the "all-or-nothing" policy, and organized resistance to the Yellowtail Dam condemnation [Doc 52, Doc 299, Doc 669].

**Joe Medicine Crow** (B.S., M.S.) compiled the definitive legal handbook on Crow laws and treaties [Doc 2], formally refused competency classification in 1949 [Doc 3], and systematically critiqued mass competency classification [Doc 595].

**Edward P. Whiteman**, Crow Tribal Council Chairman, led opposition to repeal of Section 2, publishing "The Crow Indian Sentinel" arguing that conveyances violating Section 2 were void ab initio [Doc 717], and demanding Justice Department prosecution [Doc 67].

**Eloise Whitebear Pease** conducted extensive archival research under BIA contracts, identifying forced fee patent cases, unauthorized rights-of-way, and statute of limitations claims [Doc 401, Doc 404, Doc 805, Doc 807].

## IV. SCALE OF DISPOSSESSION

The documentary evidence permits quantification of land loss through multiple channels:

**Fee patents and trust removal**: During calendar year 1953 alone, 410 fee patents covering 91,000 acres were approved to Indians across the Billings Area, with the Crow Reservation accounting for 137 fee patents (17,130.83 acres) [Doc 702]. Between 1948 and 1957, the Billings Area Office removed 1,082,263.83 acres from trust status across seven reservations [Doc 332]. By 1943, 262,942 acres on the Crow Reservation were non-Indian owned [Doc 36].

**Supervised sales**: Between 1946 and 1955, 21 supervised sales covering 517 tracts disposed of 111,315 acres at an average appraised value of $10.27/acre, yielding $1,614,622 ($14.50/acre average) [Doc 87, Doc 280]. These sales were conducted without enforcing Section 2's acreage limitations [Doc 19, Doc 79].

**Section 2 violations**: Estimates of land held in violation of acreage limitations range from 248,000 acres (71 ranchers identified by the Crow Reservation Association) [Doc 271] to 285,000 excess acres (56 violators identified by NARF in 1977) [Doc 259] to as much as 400,000 acres [Doc 316, Doc 420].

**Illegal leasing**: A 1962 Missouri River Basin Investigation report found approximately 90% of leased Crow land—roughly 800,000 acres—was probably leased illegally through "overlapping" leases [Doc 291, Doc 398, Doc 440].

**Overall land status**: By 1960, the reservation contained 1,330,562 acres of allotted land and 266,408 acres of tribal land [Doc 8]. Approximately 591,700 acres had trust restrictions released or were fee-patented by 1955 [Doc 280]. By 1962, approximately 70% of the reservation was in non-Indian hands through ownership or leasing [Doc 431].

The forced fee patent summary compiled in the Stanton Collection documents over 500 individual cases [Doc 777, Doc 817], but this represents only a fraction of total fee patents issued, as it captures only those cases identified as "forced" rather than all fee patent issuances.

## V. OUTCOMES

### A. Immediate Consequences for Individual Allottees

The superintendents' own reports acknowledged the devastating pattern: "Nearly all patents-in-fee resulted in immediate land loss through sale, mortgage default, or tax delinquency" [Doc 33]. Superintendent Estep's 1915–1917 reports catalog dozens of specific cases where approximately 90–95% of patented land was sold, with proceeds overwhelmingly squandered within days or weeks [Doc 746]. Acting Commissioner Zimmerman testified in 1949 that "95 percent of fee-patented lands were sold immediately after issuance and proceeds frittered away" [Doc 312].

Specific documented outcomes include:
- **Mary K. Reed** (Allotment No. 34): Fee patent issued August 9, 1916; by 1931, approximately $850 in delinquent taxes; by 1958, receiving reduced Old Age Assistance [Doc 655].
- **Josephine Collins Terrel** (Allotment No. 3137): Fee patent issued July 26, 1923, without her consent; land taken by Big Horn County tax deed in 1937 [Doc 763, Doc 764].
- **Frederick Geisdorff Jr.** (Allotment No. 2336): Died of influenza at age 20 before the trust period expired; despite the trust patent's reversion clause, the BIA processed the sale to a non-Indian for $1,500 [Doc 811].
- **Perry Pretty Paint**: Received six fee patents in June 1956 covering over 700 acres; immediately accumulated $9,690.61 in vehicle debt [Doc 821].
- **Phoebe Wilson Dillon** (Allotment No. 3636): Lost 1,040 acres for $3,711 in cash; the Ninth Circuit affirmed summary judgment against her, and the Supreme Court denied certiorari [Doc 257, Doc 273].

### B. Failure of Remedial Mechanisms

The Act of February 26, 1927 (44 Stat. 1247) authorized cancellation of fee patents issued without consent, but the Crow Agency superintendent reported that "most fee patents had been issued seven or eight years earlier, and in most cases the patented land had already been sold or mortgaged, limiting the act's applicability" [Doc 755].

The Department of Justice declined criminal prosecution for pre-1955 Section 2 violations and took no civil action despite referral of cases on April 6, 1960 [Doc 28, Doc 285]. On November 20, 1967, the DOJ closed both the Dillon file and the general file on Crow conveyance violations [Doc 340].

Six congressional bills between 1956 and 1964 sought to retroactively validate illegal land transfers rather than restore Crow lands [Doc 245, Doc 263, Doc 283, Doc 285, Doc 706, Doc 740]. The Crow Tribe's own lawsuit (*Crow Tribe v. Campbell Farming Corp.*, 31 F.3d 768, 9th Cir. 1994) was dismissed on the ground that Section 2 referenced "any Crow Indian"—individual allottees, not the tribe—denying the tribe standing to challenge the very land accumulation the statute was designed to prevent [Doc 258].

### C. Structural Transformation of the Reservation

The cumulative effect was the transformation of the Crow Reservation from a contiguous tribal land base into a "checkerboard" of Indian and non-Indian ownership. By the 1990s, Warren D. Smith of Pryor reported that bankers who owned substantial Crow land refused loans to Crow borrowers, viewing Indian ranchers as economic competitors [Doc 428]. The 1986 Interior Inspector General audit confirmed that BIA had never incorporated Section 2 restrictions into 25 CFR and repeatedly failed to forward test cases to the Justice Department [Doc 736].

## VI. WHAT THE DOCUMENTS PROVE

1. **Forced fee patenting was systematic, not incidental.** Over 500 individual cases are documented in the Stanton Collection alone [Doc 777, Doc 817], with concentrated batches issued on single dates (notably July 26, 1923) [Doc 266, Doc 764]. The 1919 and 1923 schedules submitted by superintendents for mass fee patenting demonstrate administrative campaigns rather than individual determinations [Doc 752, Doc 282].

2. **The BIA knowingly violated its own statutory obligations.** Between 1942 and 1955, 21 supervised sales covering 517 tracts proceeded without any reference to Section 2's acreage limitations [Doc 19, Doc 79]. The Area Director claimed Washington told him the limitations were "not operative" [Doc 28]. The BIA's own 1955 tabulation identified specific violators holding tens of thousands of acres each [Doc 413].

3. **Pre-arranged sales between non-Indian purchasers and allottees preceded fee patent issuance.** The Dillon case documents a contract executed while land was still in trust [Doc 257, Doc 293]. Superintendent Hobbs confirmed this was routine practice [Doc 8]. Attorney Stanton maintained a Washington surveillance operation to learn of patent issuances immediately [Doc 827].

4. **Specific non-Indian entities accumulated vast holdings in documented violation of federal law.** Antler Land Company held 34,195–42,565 acres [Doc 413, Doc 663]; Campbell Farming Corporation held 14,762–45,000 acres [Doc 413, Doc 258]; Homer Scott entities held approximately 67,000 acres [Doc 707]. These accumulations were known to the BIA and documented in confidential internal tabulations [Doc 413].

5. **Federal remedial mechanisms consistently failed.** The 1927 cancellation act was rendered moot by prior sales [Doc 755]. The Justice Department declined prosecution [Doc 28, Doc 340]. Congress attempted retroactive validation rather than restoration [Doc 245, Doc 740]. Courts denied tribal standing [Doc 258, Doc 259]. Missing BIA records further obscured the evidentiary trail [Doc 5, Doc 733].

6. **Attorneys serving as intermediaries profited directly from the dispossession process.** Harold Stanton simultaneously represented allottees, held mortgages on their land, purchased land at nominal consideration, and resold to non-Indians [Doc 819–827]. Bert Kronmiller served as both tribal attorney and county attorney while representing non-Indian purchasers [Doc 668].

## VII. WHAT THE DOCUMENTS SUGGEST

1. **The competency commission's "written applications" for fee patents were likely fabricated in many cases.** The Josephine Collins Terrel case—where the allottee denied applying, the patent was never delivered, yet the BIA insisted on the "presumption of regularity"—suggests a pattern of manufactured consent [Doc 763, Doc 764]. The simultaneous issuance of dozens of patents in the 913,000 series on July 26, 1923, is consistent with batch processing of a pre-prepared list rather than individual voluntary applications [Doc 777].

2. **The "all-or-nothing" policy was designed to accelerate termination of trust status.** Commissioner Emmons' articulation of the policy as enabling "the Federal Government to eliminate the responsibilities of management of trust lands" [Doc 721] suggests it served termination-era objectives rather than individual Indian interests.

3. **A coordinated network of attorneys, land companies, and BIA officials facilitated dispossession.** The Tschirgi family's operations—spanning Antler Land Company, Little Horn Land Company, and Little Horn Land and Livestock Company, financed by the Teamsters pension fund, with legal representation by Hibbs, Sweeney & Colberg—operated as an integrated land acquisition enterprise [Doc 248, Doc 251, Doc 335]. Stanton's Washington surveillance operation suggests similar systematic coordination [Doc 827].

4. **The BIA's refusal to enforce Section 2 was not mere negligence but reflected institutional alignment with non-Indian economic interests.** The Hardin Chamber of Commerce sought to facilitate land company purchases [Doc 1]. The Crow Reservation Association, representing 71 non-Indian members holding 248,000 deeded acres, collected over $22,000 to fund lobbying [Doc 271]. BIA officials falsely told field staff that Section 2 had been amended [Doc 430].

## VIII. GAPS IN THE RECORD

1. **Missing BIA records.** Box 27 of the Central Classified Files 1958–75 for the Crow Reservation is documented as "MISSING"—neither records, information, nor charge card found [Doc 5]. Archival folder covers for 1923 and 1931 land files exist but the records they should contain are absent [Doc 733]. These gaps coincide with critical periods of fee patent issuance and land sale activity.

2. **Incomplete quantification of forced fee patents.** The Stanton Collection summary of 500+ cases [Doc 777, Doc 817] represents only identified "forced" cases. The total number of fee patents issued on the Crow Reservation—including those nominally voluntary but obtained through economic coercion, misinformation, or predatory lending—cannot be determined from the available corpus.

3. **Individual allottee experiences.** While the Dillon, Terrel, Wallace, and Geisdorff cases are documented in detail, the vast majority of the 500+ forced fee patent cases lack comparable narrative records. The voices of most dispossessed allottees are absent from the archival record.

4. **Financial flows.** The documents establish that land was sold at below-market prices but rarely trace the full financial chain—what purchasers subsequently earned from the land, how much was extracted through sub-leasing, or the aggregate wealth transfer from Crow to non-Indian ownership.

5. **The 1958–1975 period.** The missing Box 27 and poor documentation of this era leave a significant gap in understanding how fee patenting and land sales operated during the transition from termination to self-determination policy.

6. **Mineral rights exploitation.** While the documents establish that mineral rights were reserved to the tribe under the 1920 Act, the actual extraction of coal, oil, and gas from formerly allotted lands—and the distribution of royalties—is poorly documented in this corpus. Peabody Coal Company's drilling of 17 core holes on Harold Male Bear's trust land without compensation [Doc 392] suggests systematic mineral trespass that remains largely unquantified.
