# Harlow Pease and His Relationship with the Crow: A Corpus-Wide Synthesis

## Identity and Professional Position

Harlow Pease appears across this corpus in a specific and consequential role: he served as **Field Solicitor** (also described as "BIA area counsel" and "Interior Department solicitor's employee") in the Department of the Interior's Office of the Solicitor, based in **Billings, Montana** [Doc 79, Doc 430, Doc 431, Doc 706]. His office was responsible for providing legal opinions on matters affecting the Crow Reservation and other Montana reservations administered through the BIA's Billings Area Office. The surname "Pease" is a recognized Crow family name—multiple Crow allottees named Pease appear throughout the record (George Pease Sr., Anson Harold Pease, Wallace W. Pease, Eloise Whitebear Pease, Weien Pease Wolf, Helen Pease Wolf, and others) [Doc 339, Doc 647, Doc 392, Doc 661, Doc 777, Doc 804]—though the documents do not explicitly confirm Harlow Pease's tribal enrollment status. His name appears on the 1923 fee patent schedule with a spelling discrepancy ("Ashable Pease vs. Ashabale Pease," allotment #1325) [Doc 266], and the "Summary of Forced Fee Patent Cases" lists multiple Pease family members who lost land through forced fee patents [Doc 777, Doc 817]. Eloise Whitebear Pease, the prolific Crow researcher and land rights advocate whose collection forms a substantial portion of this archive, may have been related to him, though the documents do not make this connection explicit.

## Harlow Pease's Central Role: Discovering and Exposing Section 2 Violations

The most significant thread connecting Harlow Pease to the Crow and to Section 2 of the Crow Act is his role as **the person who discovered—and repeatedly warned about—the Bureau of Indian Affairs' systematic failure to enforce Section 2's acreage limitations**.

### The Discovery

Section 2 of the Crow Allotment Act of June 4, 1920 (41 Stat. 751), prohibited the Secretary of the Interior from approving any conveyance of Crow allotted land to a person, company, or corporation already owning 640 acres of agricultural land or 1,280 acres of grazing land within the Crow Reservation. It further capped total acquisitions at 1,280 acres agricultural or 1,920 acres grazing, with violations declared void and punishable by fines up to $5,000 or six months imprisonment [Doc 19, Doc 23, Doc 79].

Senator Murray's 1956 statement to the Senate reveals that **"an attorney in the Field Solicitor's office in Billings, Montana, discovered the situation"** regarding decades of unenforced acreage limitations, which prompted Commissioner Glenn L. Emmons to suspend all Crow Reservation land sales and order an investigation [Doc 245]. Multiple documents confirm this attorney was Harlow Pease. The Billings Gazette reported in August 1962 that **"Billings attorney Harlow Pease, formerly BIA area counsel and Interior Department solicitor's employee, claimed he repeatedly informed Bureau of Indian Affairs officials that they were ignoring provisions of the Crow Act of 1920, specifically Section 2"** [Doc 430]. The Great Falls Tribune similarly reported that **"Pease stated he had repeatedly warned BIA officials of their neglect"** [Doc 431].

### The Timeline of Pease's Warnings

The documentary record establishes that Pease's discovery and warnings occurred in the **fall of 1955**. The BIA's own Area Realty Officer confirmed in an October 4, 1955 memo that the acreage limitation provision had been **"disregarded" since the Area Office's establishment** [Doc 413]. A confidential October 27, 1955 tabulation by Area Director J.M. Cooper to Associate Commissioner H. Rex Lee—prepared in response to the discovery—identified over two dozen non-Indian landholders exceeding statutory limits, including Antler Land Company/M.H. & F.M. Tschirgi at **34,195.75 acres**, Campbell Farming Corporation at **14,762.53 acres**, Henman-Kelly at **33,303.28 acres**, and Warren & Sons at **22,505.60 acres** [Doc 413].

Commissioner Emmons suspended all Crow land sales in late 1955 upon learning of the violations [Doc 19, Doc 23, Doc 79, Doc 245].

### BIA Officials' Response to Pease

The documents reveal a striking pattern: BIA officials **denied knowledge of the law and claimed it had been amended**, directly contradicting Pease's warnings. The Billings Gazette reported that **"BIA officials told Pease the law had been amended to revoke these provisions, which Pease flatly denied after checking the U.S. Code"** [Doc 430]. At the 1957 House subcommittee hearings at Crow Agency, attorney John M. Dietrich testified that the law was ignored because **"the U.S. Code Annotated omitted reference to Section 2"** [Doc 430]. The BIA's Billings Area Director claimed **"Washington told him the limitations were 'not operative'"** [Doc 28, Doc 285]. Acting Assistant Solicitor William V. Koetler subsequently confirmed that **Section 2 had never been repealed** [Doc 413].

Assistant Secretary Roger Ernst acknowledged in a March 1960 letter that **"no adequate explanation of the circumstances is available"** for the enforcement failure [Doc 430]. At congressional hearings, BIA representatives admitted they could offer **"no explanation whatsoever"** [Doc 263, Doc 283].

## Pease's Legal Opinions on Section 2

Beyond discovering the violations, Pease authored substantive legal opinions interpreting Section 2's provisions:

### The May 23, 1957 Field Solicitor Opinion

Pease's **May 23, 1957 opinion** addressed the interpretation of the acreage limitation statute. He **construed "or" as "and"** in the statute, permitting diversified agricultural-and-grazing operations but maintaining category-specific ceilings [Doc 79]. This opinion was significant because it determined how the acreage caps would be applied going forward after the suspension of sales.

However, this opinion was later **overruled** by the Solicitor's office in Washington. The **December 7, 1959 Solicitor's opinion** (G-59-1139.9), addressing the specific case of Jo Annette Hammond—who owned 1,435.08 acres of grazing land and 160 acres of agricultural land—held that **exceeding the ceiling in either category barred acquisition of both classes** [Doc 23, Doc 79]. This was a stricter interpretation than Pease's, meaning that a person who exceeded the grazing limit could not purchase even agricultural land, and vice versa.

### The R.B. Fraser Trespass Case

Pease also applied Section 2 principles in the context of land sales to known trespassers. His **November 4, 1954 memorandum** to Area Director John M. Cooper documented twelve years of R.B. Fraser's livestock trespasses on Crow grazing units (Units 18, 19, 20, 20A, 22, 23), with **$2,506.00 in accumulated unpaid penalties**, citing 25 USC 179. Pease recommended **rejecting Fraser's bids** on ten tracts totaling 2,340.94 acres at $33,651.62, noting the elder Fraser used his son's name as a **"device"** to circumvent his persona non grata status. However, M.A. Johnson's November 5 memo to Cooper found no regulatory authority to reject bids based on pending trespass litigation, and the bids were apparently processed [Doc 409].

## Pease's Advocacy for Individual Crow Allottees

### The Phoebe Wilson Dillon Case

Pease's most sustained advocacy on behalf of an individual Crow allottee appears in the **Dillon v. Antler Land Company** litigation. The Department of Justice litigation case file (90-2-5-355) documents that **"Attorney Harlow Pease argued the contract was 'absolutely null and void' under the statute"**—referring to the May 21, 1955 contract by which Phoebe Wilson Dillon agreed to sell her 1,040-acre allotment (No. 3636) to Antler Land Company for $7,280 while the land was still in trust [Doc 340].

Pease's legal argument rested on two grounds:
1. The pre-patent contract violated **Section 5 of the General Allotment Act (25 U.S.C. § 348)**, which rendered void any contract touching allotted land before expiration of the trust period.
2. The conveyance violated **Section 2 of the 1920 Crow Act**, because Antler Land Company already owned far more than 1,920 acres of grazing land within the reservation—the company held over **40,000 acres** [Doc 413, Doc 663].

The documents reveal that Pease was a **persistent advocate** in this case. The March 6, 1971 Request for Admission of Facts notes that **"despite the BIA Area Director's September 11, 1963 recommendation urging suit, and attorney Harlow Pease's persistent advocacy, the Acting Associate Solicitor's August 11, 1964 memorandum demanded additional proof of fraud beyond statutory illegality, effectively stalling federal action"** [Doc 293]. The DOJ ultimately closed the file on November 20, 1967, without bringing suit [Doc 340].

### The Ballensky Investigation

Pease was also involved in investigating exploitative lending to Crow members. A **May 26, 1953 letter to Area Counsel Harlow Pease** from Superintendent L.C. Lippert detailed specific allotment parcels in Township 6 South, Range 31 East that were Indian trust lands under lease to A.G. Ballensky of Hardin, Montana, who had made unauthorized loans to Crow members based on anticipated fee patents [Doc 76]. Lippert suspected Ballensky was attempting to force the agency to collect debts from Indian income and recommended referral to the Department of Justice.

## Pease's Role in the Broader Legislative and Political Struggle

### The 1957 Hearings

At the October 18-19, 1957 House subcommittee hearings held on the Crow Reservation, the consequences of Pease's discovery were front and center. The hearings documented that between approximately 1946 and January 1956, roughly **111,815 acres** of restricted Crow land were sold under Bureau supervision without any reference to Section 2 in sale notices [Doc 271]. The Crow Reservation Association, representing 71 non-Indian members holding approximately **248,000 deeded acres**, had collected over **$22,000** to fund lobbying and legal efforts to repeal Section 2 [Doc 271].

### The 1962 Newspaper Coverage

By 1962, Pease had left government service and was speaking publicly. The Billings Gazette's Dick Gilluly reported that Pease **"claimed he repeatedly informed Bureau of Indian Affairs officials that they were ignoring provisions of the Crow Act of 1920"** and that BIA officials falsely told him the law had been amended [Doc 430]. The article also noted that **Attorney General Robert F. Kennedy was reportedly interested in the legal status of Crow lands operated by non-Indians** and that the **Justice Department had been sitting on two cases since 1960 awaiting BIA referral** [Doc 430].

### The 1964 Compromise Debate

A January 12, 1964 Billings Gazette column by Dick Gilluly references **"Pease" who "cites several instances"** of inadequate compensation in land transactions, in the context of the Metcalf-Mansfield bill to validate illegal land transactions [Doc 416, Doc 437]. The article notes that **"a comprehensive study of all transactions had never been conducted"** and that Pease and Justice Department officials cited specific instances where Indians definitively did not receive fair market value [Doc 437].

### The July 4, 1962 Memorandum

A detailed memorandum by Harlow Pease dated **July 4, 1962**, preserved in Senator Mansfield's papers, documents the systematic dispossession of Crow Indian land. The memorandum details how **"the BIA conspired with non-Indian ranchers to ignore these restrictions, enabling massive illegal accumulations—specifically M.H. Tschirgi (Antler Land Co.) holding over 40,000 acres and Campbell Farming Corporation over 25,000 acres."** It states that approximately **70 percent of the reservation passed to non-Indian control**, that **191,751 acres (80 percent) were under overlapping leases** as of December 19, 1957, and that between **300,000 and 500,000 acres** were estimated held under legally void fee patents [Doc 715].

### The S. 3678 Legislative Record

The 1962 legislative record for S. 3678 notes that **"Former BIA counsel Harlow Pease publicly stated he had warned officials of violations but was ignored"** [Doc 706]. The Interior Department's response suggested Congress consider repealing the acreage limitations entirely—effectively advocating removal of the sole statutory protection against land consolidation by non-Indian ranchers.

## Pease's Relationship to the "All or Nothing" Fee Patent Policy

One document suggests Pease also weighed in on the BIA's controversial "all or nothing" fee patent policy. Bill Shaw, Postmaster at Lodge Grass, reported in January 1955 that **"attorney Marlow Pease had advised BIA officials this practice was legally unauthorized, but they persisted"** [Doc 795]. The "all or nothing" policy required Indian allottees applying for a fee patent on any portion of their allotted lands to simultaneously take a patent on all holdings—a policy that Senator Murray's office characterized as contradicting congressional intent and effectively pushing Indian trust land onto tax rolls against allottees' wishes [Doc 795].

## Pease's Opinions on Competent Leasing

The documents also reference Pease's legal analysis of "competent leases"—the mechanism by which non-Indian lessees maintained perpetual control over Crow allotments. The BIA records note that **"extensive solicitor opinions by Harlow Pease address how 'competent leases' by entities like the Antler Land Company were used to block supervised sales, creating perpetual leaseholds that further dispossessed Indian landowners"** [Doc 413].

## What the Documents Prove

1. **Harlow Pease was the Field Solicitor who discovered the BIA's systematic non-enforcement of Section 2 of the Crow Act in the fall of 1955**, prompting the suspension of all Crow land sales [Doc 245, Doc 430, Doc 431].

2. **Pease repeatedly warned BIA officials that they were violating the law**, and was told—falsely—that Section 2 had been amended or was inoperative [Doc 430, Doc 431, Doc 706].

3. **Pease authored the May 23, 1957 Field Solicitor opinion** interpreting Section 2's acreage limitations, which was later partially overruled by the Washington Solicitor's office [Doc 79].

4. **Pease persistently advocated for Phoebe Wilson Dillon**, arguing her land sale to Antler Land Company was "absolutely null and void" under both the General Allotment Act and Section 2, but was overruled by the Associate Solicitor's office, which demanded additional proof of fraud [Doc 293, Doc 340].

5. **Pease investigated and documented specific violations**, including the R.B. Fraser trespass case [Doc 409], the Ballensky unauthorized lending scheme [Doc 76], and the Antler Land Company's massive illegal accumulations [Doc 413, Doc 715].

6. **Pease advised against the BIA's "all or nothing" fee patent policy** as legally unauthorized [Doc 795].

7. **After leaving government service, Pease spoke publicly** about the BIA's failures, providing evidence to journalists and legislators that helped sustain political pressure for enforcement [Doc 430, Doc 431, Doc 437, Doc 706, Doc 715].

8. **The scale of violations Pease uncovered was enormous**: over two dozen non-Indian landholders exceeding statutory limits, with individual holdings reaching 34,000+ acres; approximately 111,315 acres sold in violation between 1946-1955 alone; and an estimated 300,000-500,000 acres held under legally void conveyances [Doc 413, Doc 271, Doc 715].

## What the Documents Suggest

1. **Pease may have been of Crow descent.** The Pease surname appears repeatedly among Crow allottees (George Pease Sr., allottee #1323; Anson Harold Pease, allottee #1322; Wallace W. Pease; Eloise Whitebear Pease), and the 1923 fee patent schedule includes "Ashable Pease" (allotment #1325) [Doc 266, Doc 777]. If Harlow Pease was a Crow tribal member serving as Field Solicitor, his advocacy takes on additional dimensions of personal and communal stake in the outcome. The forced fee patent summary lists George Pease Sr. as having received forced patents on 320 acres [Doc 404], suggesting the Pease family directly experienced the dispossession mechanisms Harlow Pease later sought to combat.

2. **Pease's warnings were deliberately ignored rather than merely overlooked.** The pattern—BIA officials claiming the law had been amended when it had not, the Area Realty Officer acknowledging the provision had been "disregarded" since the office's establishment, and the Washington office telling the Area Director compliance "was not necessary"—suggests institutional complicity rather than mere negligence [Doc 413, Doc 718].

3. **Pease's departure from government service may have been connected to his advocacy.** The documents show him as "formerly BIA area counsel" by 1962 [Doc 430], and his increasingly public criticism of the BIA after leaving suggests possible friction between his legal conclusions and institutional preferences.

4. **Pease's legal analysis, had it been heeded, could have prevented decades of dispossession.** His discovery came in 1955, but the violations had been ongoing since at least 1942 (and arguably since 1921). Had the BIA enforced Section 2 as Pease insisted, the massive land accumulations by Antler Land Company, Campbell Farming Corporation, and others would have been prevented or reversed.

## Gaps in the Record

1. **Pease's personal biography is largely absent.** The documents do not confirm his tribal enrollment status, educational background, dates of service as Field Solicitor, or circumstances of his departure from government.

2. **The full text of Pease's internal memoranda and legal opinions is mostly unavailable.** The corpus references his opinions but rarely reproduces them in full. The July 4, 1962 memorandum [Doc 715] is the most complete surviving statement, but it was written after he left government service.

3. **Pease's relationship to Eloise Whitebear Pease is undocumented.** Given the shared surname and shared commitment to Crow land rights, a familial connection seems plausible but cannot be confirmed from these records.

4. **The BIA's internal response to Pease's warnings is incompletely documented.** We know the outcome (continued non-enforcement until the Commissioner intervened in late 1955), but the internal deliberations—who decided to ignore Pease, and why—are not fully captured.

5. **Pease's role in the 1957 Crow Agency hearings is unclear.** He is not listed as a witness in the hearing transcript [Doc 271], though his discovery precipitated the hearings. Whether he testified, was excluded, or chose not to participate is not documented.

6. **Missing BIA records may contain additional Pease materials.** Document 5 records that a box of Crow Agency records from 1958-75 (CCF File 5497, Box 27) has been marked **"MISSING"**—neither records, information, nor charge card found—raising questions about whether evidence of Pease's advocacy or the BIA's response was lost or removed from federal custody.
