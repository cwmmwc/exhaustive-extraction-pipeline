Deep Reading Analysis: Board of Indian Commissioners Reports on Patent-in-Fee Land Loss (1921)

Document Identification and Context

This is a compilation document assembled by the Board of Indian Commissioners in 1921, consisting of multiple bulletins (Nos. 138, 140, 141, 142, 143, 144, 145, 147, 150, 152) containing field reports from superintendents, agency farmers, field clerks, and at least one Native American correspondent. The compilation was transmitted in batches between approximately April and October 1921 to the Commissioner of Indian Affairs (Charles H. Burke), with cover letters signed by Malcolm McDowell, Secretary of the Board. The document represents one of the most comprehensive grassroots surveys of the consequences of the fee patent policy ever assembled, drawing on 87 replies from reservations and field clerk districts across Washington, Idaho, Montana, Wyoming, North Dakota, South Dakota, Wisconsin, Kansas, and Oklahoma.

The document's date in the filename (c. 1921) is confirmed by internal evidence, though some cover letters bear the date 1931—likely OCR errors for 1921, as the content, personnel, and policy references are consistent with 1921. The reference to Chairman Vaux and Secretary McDowell visiting Commissioner Burke and discussing the patent-in-fee question as "of supreme importance" establishes this as a deliberate policy investigation.

The Document's Purpose and Narrative Arc

The Board of Indian Commissioners initiated this survey in response to growing alarm about the consequences of the "Declaration of Policy" of 1917, under which over 17,000 patents-in-fee had been issued to Indians. The questionnaire sent to field personnel asked three fundamental questions:

  1. What happened to Indians who received fee patents?
  2. Are we going too fast in releasing Indians from government supervision?
  3. What policy would you recommend?

The document's narrative arc moves from bureaucratic inquiry to a devastating accumulation of evidence. Report after report, from geographically dispersed reservations with different tribal populations and economic conditions, arrives at remarkably similar conclusions: the fee patent policy resulted in massive, rapid, and largely irreversible land loss, with the overwhelming majority of patentees selling or mortgaging their land within months of receiving their patents, spending the proceeds (primarily on automobiles), and ending up destitute or dependent on relatives who still held restricted land.

The Mechanism of Land Loss: A Systematic Analysis

The Fee Patent as Instrument of Dispossession

The document reveals that the fee patent functioned not as a tool of Indian empowerment but as a mechanism for transferring Indian land to white ownership. Superintendent Stinchecum of the Kiowa Agency articulated this most precisely:

"In my experience here, during a period of more than six years, I cannot now recall but one case where the Indian has made a success in the handling of his land... taking the last sixty patents issued as the result of the Declaration of Policy, the records of the various County Seats show that of this number twenty were promptly mortgaged. Of this twenty only one of them has been released. Sixteen tracts have been sold outright."

This passage is extraordinary because it provides hard data from county records—not impressions but documented transactions. Of sixty patents, thirty-six (60%) were immediately encumbered or sold, with the remaining twenty-four described as "only a question of time."

The Speed of Dispossession

The rapidity of land loss is one of the document's most striking findings. Superintendent Leech at Yankton reported:

"Of the 613 who have received patents to a part or all of their allotments, practically 550 have disposed of all of their land and of this number probably 400 have absolutely nothing to show for it."

At Rosebud, Superintendent Buntin estimated that "nine out of ten sold their land, spent the proceeds derived therefrom, and were very little better off within one or two years from the time their patent-in-fee was received."

At the Coeur d'Alene Agency, Superintendent Lawshe reported: "I presume 98% of the 373 Indians who have been granted certificates of competency have sold their land."

The Fort Peck farmer reported: "Every Indian in my district who has received a patent-in-fee for his original allotment has failed."

The Role of White Predators

The document consistently identifies a predatory infrastructure of white land speculators, loan agents, and automobile dealers who systematically targeted Indians upon receipt of fee patents. Superintendent Stinchecum described the dynamic:

"in about 99 out of 100 cases the patent is never asked for till the applicant has studied up some plan to alienate it. He never keeps it long enough to teach him the first principles of business independence."

The encounter described by Farmer Edmister at Shawnee is particularly revealing. A loan and insurance agent approached him asking about when specific Indians would receive their patents, demonstrating that white speculators were actively tracking the patent process:

"Just as I touched the pavement a gentleman with whom I was well acquainted, and who represents a loan and insurance agency, came to me and mentioning a certain Indian, asked how soon he would get his patent."

Edmister concluded: "In this instance all plans had evidently been made to eventually alienate this land before the application was made."

R.R. Youngblood, working among Creek and Seminole Indians, described the grafters explicitly:

"In the Indian country the land men - commonly called grafters - are demanding that the Indians be turned loose in order that the country may develop and taxes be lowered... The honest hard-working farmers living among the Indians do not want them turned loose, as they have seen the result of turning a few loose."

The Automobile as Instrument of Dispossession

The automobile emerges as the single most important consumer good through which land wealth was converted to nothing. Superintendent Leech described the typical pattern:

"Immediately upon the receipt of a patent in fee the Indian is beset by some white man or a number of them who wish to purchase his land, sometimes at prices considerably below the adequate, and sell them automobiles or something the Indian has no particular need of. They fall for this, and when next we see the Indian he and his family are riding around in some car much more expensive than the average white man can afford."

Superintendent Lawshe at Coeur d'Alene made a remarkable observation: "From an economic standpoint, the automobile is nearly as demoralising to the Indian as whiskey."

The automobile was not merely a luxury purchase but functioned as a mechanism for rapid capital depletion—requiring ongoing expenses for gasoline, repairs, and eventually replacement, creating a downward spiral that consumed land sale proceeds within months.

The Kaw Sub-Agency Case Studies: Granular Evidence

Clerk John W. Clendening's detailed case-by-case listing of Kaw Indians provides the most granular evidence in the document. His 34 years of service lent authority to his observations. The individual entries reveal a devastating pattern:

  • Margaret Tayiah, age 30, 4/4 blood: "Land sold, money spent, lives with Osage"
  • Helen Jones Burnett, age 34, 4/4 blood: "Land sold. Husband and money both gone"
  • Claude McCauley, age 46, 4/4 blood: "Land sold, money gone. Has 160 inherited"
  • Harry Stubbs, age 47, 4/4 blood: "Land sold. Funds gone. Living off his friends"
  • Barclay Delano, age 31, 4/4 blood: "Land sold, money gone. Red Cross and neighbors aid for him"

The pattern is relentless. Of approximately 65 individuals listed, the vast majority show "Land sold. Money spent/gone." The few exceptions are instructive—those who retained land tended to be mixed-bloods married to white spouses, or older individuals with established farms.

Clendening's summary is devastating: "I have some 25 Kaws who are perfectly competent, but they have not asked for their certificates... It is usually the incompetent who makes the most effort to get the restrictions removed."

The Voice of Samuel Charger: An Indigenous Perspective

One of the most significant passages in the entire document is the letter from Samuel Charger, a Sioux Indian from La Plant, South Dakota. His is the only clearly identified Native voice speaking from personal experience as a member of the affected community:

"There is over five hundred Indians on this reservation who have been released from the Government control, out of this number very few Indians have made good... As a rule we Indians are large land owners and some have the best of locations and well improved allotments, once we own lands and have homes, and are in the prime of life to make some progress but today because we are released from Government control we have disposed of our allotments and our homes and some of us are homeless and are in a very sad plight."

Charger's use of "we" is significant—he includes himself among those affected. His analysis is sophisticated:

"My contention is that the Department is going too fast in releasing us Indians from the Federal control. I am also opposed to the granting of citizenship to Indians in the bulk."

He specifically addresses the American Legion's advocacy for Indian citizenship for veterans: "The American Legion says that all ex Indian soldiers should be citizens, and today the majority of our ex-service boys are without lands and are therefore not independent citizens. Such being a very bad example to the tribe."

This passage directly contradicts the narrative that Indian veterans' service demonstrated their competency for citizenship and land management. Charger argues the opposite—that veterans who received patents lost their land and became negative examples.

The Paradox of Competency

One of the document's most intellectually significant findings is what Superintendent Lipps at Nez Perce called "the incompetency of the competent and the competency of the incompetent":

"as far as we are able to observe the 'prima facie competent' are the first to dispose of their lands and squander the proceeds... The older people who have received fee patents, most of whom are uneducated, are, as a rule holding onto their lands and many of them are buying other lands."

This paradox undermined the entire theoretical basis of the competency system. The educated young Indians—those whom the government had invested in through schooling—were the most likely to sell their land. The uneducated older Indians, who would have been classified as incompetent by any standard measure, were the most successful at retaining and even expanding their holdings.

Lipps explained the mechanism: "The older people grew up under different conditions. They had to work and save, or starve... The younger generation have been educated in the Government schools where everything was furnished them free of cost. They have never had to exert themselves."

This insight reveals a fundamental flaw in the assimilationist logic: government education, by providing everything free, actually undermined the economic discipline that the older generation had developed through necessity.

The Structural Trap: Allotment Geography

Superintendent Kitoh's analysis of Standing Rock reveals a structural dimension of land loss that goes beyond individual competency. Children allotted in 1908-1909 received land 50-70 miles from their parents' homes:

"These Indians who were then children were forced to take allotments many miles from their parents' home and grow up with little or no knowledge of the land allotted to them... In order to take advantage of these lands as their homes they would have been forced to have left their neighbors and relatives."

This geographic dispersal of allotments meant that many Indians had no practical use for their allotted land. Selling it was not irresponsible—it was rational. But the proceeds were consumed rather than reinvested, because the Indians lacked the business infrastructure and experience to convert cash into productive assets.

Kitoh further noted that railroad construction after allotment had made the children's distant allotments far more valuable than the parents' riverside allotments: "the land allotted to children who are today's young men and young women would have an average value of twenty dollars per acre, some selling as high as thirty dollars" while the parents' land "has a value of not exceeding ten dollars." This meant the youngest, least experienced Indians held the most valuable land—a structural invitation to dispossession.

The Five Civilized Tribes: A Different but Parallel Pattern

The reports from Oklahoma's Five Civilized Tribes reveal a parallel pattern operating under different legal frameworks. The Act of May 27, 1908 had already removed restrictions on mixed-blood Indians, and the results were catastrophic.

Youngblood's quantification of losses in Hughes and Seminole Counties is remarkable:

"In Hughes County there were sixteen hundred mixed blood allotments of one hundred sixty acres each, appraised value \$1,040 each... 75 per cent of the mixed-blood Indians of Hughes County were defrauded out of seventy per cent of the value of their allotments."

His calculation: 1,200 Indians × \$728 loss each = \$873,600 in Hughes County alone. In Seminole County: 567 × \$960 = \$544,320. Combined losses of \$1,417,920 (equivalent to approximately \$22 million in 2024 dollars) from just two counties.

The nine specific fraud cases he documents—Mollie Johnson, Daniel Johnson, Adeline Grayson, Caney Arbor, Henderson Arbor, Talof Harjo, Lizzie Sewell, Tilda Sunday, Jemima Harjo—provide concrete evidence of the mechanisms of fraud: Indians "deluded," "prevailed upon," "persuaded," and "induced" to sign conveyances for fractions of their land's value.

What the AI Extraction Missed

1. The Moral and Philosophical Arguments

The AI extraction captured entities and transactions but missed the sophisticated moral reasoning throughout the document. Farmer Edmister at Shawnee articulated a philosophy of property rights:

"Private property, as represented here by the Indian allotments, has always been one of the most stable institutions of civilization. It is the basis, and one of the greatest incentives of all business... its great importance is shown by the condition of Russia to day, where it has been ignored."

This 1921 reference to the Russian Revolution as a cautionary tale about property rights is historically significant—it shows how field-level Indian Service employees were framing Indian policy within broader ideological debates about property and socialism.

2. The Critique of the Leasing System

Multiple respondents identified the leasing system itself as a cause of dependency. Superintendent Lipps made the most pointed observation:

"On all of these Northwest reservations it is the sweat of the white man that produces the bread to feed the Indians. True the Indian owns the land, but he does not work it. He leases it to white men and lives off his rentals."

This critique reveals a fundamental contradiction in allotment policy: the system was supposed to make Indians into farmers, but the leasing provisions allowed them to become rentiers instead, creating dependency without building skills.

3. The Reimbursable Fund Alternative

Edmister proposed an alternative to fee patents that the AI extraction did not capture: the "reimbursable agreement," essentially a government-backed loan system:

"if the Government is the only one who can safely collect from the Indians, and at the same time keep their property in tact, then they ought to extend him credit in this way, in cases of worthy need."

This represents a road not taken in Indian policy—a system that would have provided capital access without requiring land alienation.

4. The Gender Dimension

The document contains significant evidence about gendered patterns of land loss that the extraction partially captured but did not synthesize. Superintendent Hutchison at Shoshone noted:

"Great caution should be exercised in the granting of patents-in-fee to females, as it has been my observation that no matter how competent a woman appears, even though married to a white man, her property is soon squandered by her husband, desertions are frequent, and the woman is usually left with several small children without means of support."

Multiple Kaw cases confirm this pattern—women's land was often controlled and dissipated by husbands, and when the money ran out, the husbands left. The case of Dadie Pappan is illustrative: "Land sold. When money was spent, husband left. Married again, husband Theodore Sumner, got certificate, bought house, and deeded to Dadie. Rest spent."

5. The Kinship Economy

The document reveals an indigenous economic system based on kinship obligations that the fee patent policy disrupted. Superintendent Hart described it:

"it being one of the essentials of Indian hospitality that the visitor must be fed, regardless of the length of his stay... so-called squaw men on the Cheyenne River Reservation... told me that when they brought in large supplies... they were obliged to cache the greater part, as the wife's relatives would come visiting and stay until the visible supply was exhausted."

This kinship economy meant that individual wealth accumulation was structurally difficult—resources were redistributed through social obligations. The fee patent system, premised on individual property rights, collided with this communal economic logic. When one family member received cash from a land sale, the kinship network absorbed it.

6. The Rocky Boy Comparison

Superintendent Mossman at Fort Peck made a powerful comparative argument that the extraction missed:

"In this State are what are called the Rocky Boy Indians who roamed about this State for sixty years after other Indians had been placed on reservations. Those Rocky Boy Indians had every opportunity to become property holders and citizens... After sixty years of this freedom they had come to such a point of degradation and poverty that the people of the State petitioned the Government to provide a reservation."

This directly refuted the argument that removing government supervision would lead to Indian self-sufficiency. The Rocky Boy example showed that sixty years of "freedom" without support produced worse outcomes than the reservation system.

The Forty-Acre Homestead Proposal

Perhaps the most significant policy recommendation to emerge from the document is the near-universal call for retaining a homestead portion of allotments in trust. This recommendation appears in reports from virtually every reservation:

  • Stinchecum (Kiowa): "make every Indian select forty acres of his original allotment as a homestead and by legislative enactment declare that that forty acres could not be sold or otherwise encumbered"
  • Charger (Sioux): "Where an Indian has an established home forty acres should be held in trust for him"
  • Mills (Chickasaw): "reserve, or continue restricted, forty acres of average land from which death alone would remove the restrictions"
  • Archerd (Creek/Seminole): "each Indian should be compelled to retain forty acres of his best land for homestead"
  • Multiple others recommending 20-160 acres

The consistency of this recommendation across different tribes, regions, and perspectives suggests it represented a genuine consensus among field workers. That this recommendation was largely not adopted represents one of the great policy failures documented here.

Superintendent Hutchison at Shoshone offered the most cogent counter-argument:

"reservation of 10, 20, or 40 acres for a home would appear to leave the Indian in a state of part bond and part free, and his independent status is indefinitely postponed."

This tension between protection and paternalism was never resolved.

Silences and Omissions

1. The Voice of White Buyers

The document never includes the perspective of white land purchasers, speculators, or mortgage lenders. Their motivations, methods, and profits are described only from the outside. A complete picture would require their records.

2. Tax Revenue Implications

While several respondents mention taxation, the document does not systematically address how much tax revenue states and counties gained from converted Indian lands. This fiscal incentive for dispossession is implied but never quantified.

3. The Oil Factor

Oil and gas revenues are mentioned in passing (Mike Roy's oil money, lands near oil wells on the Crow reservation) but the document does not systematically address how mineral wealth affected the dynamics of fee patents and land loss. This would become a much larger issue in the 1920s, particularly among the Osage.

4. Legal Remedies

The document contains almost no discussion of legal remedies for fraud. While fraud is extensively documented, there is no mention of prosecutions, civil suits, or other legal mechanisms to recover stolen land or punish grafters.

5. The Competency Commission's Methods

While the competency commission is frequently criticized, the document provides little detail about how competency hearings actually worked—what questions were asked, what evidence was considered, how long hearings lasted. Stinchecum's reference to "a talk of twenty minutes" suggests the process was cursory.

Evidentiary Value

This document constitutes primary evidence of the first order for understanding the relationship between fee patents and land loss. Its value derives from:

  1. Breadth: 87 respondents across multiple states and tribal jurisdictions
  2. Specificity: Named individuals with documented outcomes
  3. Consistency: Remarkably similar findings across diverse contexts
  4. Authority: Written by government employees with direct knowledge
  5. Contemporaneity: Written while the process was ongoing, not retrospectively
  6. Candor: Many respondents explicitly criticize government policy

The document proves that: - Fee patents resulted in land loss rates of 60-98% depending on the reservation - Land loss typically occurred within months of patent issuance - The proceeds were overwhelmingly consumed rather than invested - A predatory infrastructure of white speculators actively facilitated dispossession - The competency system failed to identify Indians who would retain their land - Field-level government employees recognized the policy's failure in real time - The policy continued despite this recognition

The document suggests that: - The fee patent policy functioned as a mechanism for transferring Indian land to white ownership regardless of its stated intent - The Declaration of Policy of 1917 accelerated an already destructive process - Education and blood quantum were poor predictors of economic outcomes - The kinship economy and allotment geography created structural conditions for land loss - A homestead retention policy could have mitigated the worst outcomes

Research Leads

  1. County land records for the specific reservations mentioned would allow verification of the land transfer data and identification of the white purchasers and their networks.

  2. Competency Commission records would reveal the actual decision-making process and whether commissioners were influenced by outside interests.

  3. Declaration of Policy of 1917 and its administrative implementation—who drafted it, what data informed it, and whether the consequences described here were anticipated.

  4. The 1931 restriction termination for the Five Civilized Tribes—whether Congress extended restrictions as multiple respondents urged, and what happened if they did not.

  5. Automobile dealer records in reservation border towns—these businesses appear to have been key intermediaries in the land-to-cash-to-nothing pipeline.

  6. The Board of Indian Commissioners' summary and digest that McDowell mentioned preparing—this document would synthesize the findings and may have influenced subsequent policy.

  7. Commissioner Burke's response to these reports—whether and how they influenced his administration of Indian affairs.

  8. Samuel Charger's other writings and activities—as a literate Sioux Indian who opposed the fee patent policy, his perspective would be valuable for understanding indigenous resistance to dispossession.

  9. The Nez Perce mortgage holders—the full-blood Nez Perce woman holding \$14,000 in mortgages on white men's farms represents a counter-narrative of Indian financial sophistication that deserves further investigation.

  10. The "Indian Rights" organizations mentioned by Cope at Crow—identifying which organizations were advocating for blanket removal of restrictions and their relationship to land speculation interests.

This document, read in its entirety, constitutes a damning indictment of the fee patent system written by the government's own employees. That the policy continued and even accelerated despite this evidence speaks to the power of the economic interests driving dispossession and the political weakness of those who recognized its consequences.
