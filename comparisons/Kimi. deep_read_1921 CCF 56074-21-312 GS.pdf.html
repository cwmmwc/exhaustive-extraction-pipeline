Deep Reading Analysis: Board of Indian Commissioners Reports on Patent-in-Fee Land Loss (1921)

Document Identification and Context

This document is a compilation of field reports solicited by the Board of Indian Commissioners in early 1921, gathered through a systematic survey of superintendents, agency farmers, and field clerks across Indian country. The compilation was assembled by Secretary Malcolm McDowell and transmitted in batches to the Commissioner of Indian Affairs between May and October 1921. The document contains Bulletins No. 138, 140, 141, 142, 143, 144, 145, 147, 150, and 152, plus cover letters and a standalone report from James B. Kitch dated July 9, 1921.

The document's purpose was explicitly stated: to gather "first hand information" to help Congress and the Department "arrive at a definite policy covering the whole problem of releasing Indian wards from federal control and supervision." It represents one of the most comprehensive grassroots assessments of the patent-in-fee policy ever assembled, drawing on 87 replies from field personnel across Washington, Idaho, Montana, Wyoming, North Dakota, South Dakota, Wisconsin, Kansas, and Oklahoma.

The Kiowa Experience: Direct Evidence

The document contains one critically important letter directly addressing the Kiowa experience, authored by V. Stinchecum, Superintendent of the Kiowa Indian Agency at Anadarko, Oklahoma (Bulletin No. 145, pages 37-38). This letter is remarkable for both its candor and its statistical precision regarding the catastrophic effects of fee patents on Kiowa and affiliated tribal lands.

Stinchecum's Core Testimony

Stinchecum opens with an apology for delay, then delivers what amounts to a devastating indictment of the patent-in-fee policy as applied to his jurisdiction:

"I have given the matter of patents-in-fee very considerable thought. To me the most important administrative function of any Indian superintendent is the making of a decision as to whether he should or should not recommend favorably upon any application for a patent-in-fee. The allotment of an Indian is usually his entire estate. To grant him control thereof prematurely not only subjects him to temptation to rid himself of this estate but gives him the opportunity, through the sale thereof, to acquire extravagant habits and ruin for his later life."

This passage reveals Stinchecum's understanding that for Kiowa allottees, the allotment was not merely a piece of real estate but constituted "his entire estate" — the totality of their economic foundation. The superintendent recognized that premature fee patenting was not simply a risk of land loss but a mechanism for comprehensive economic destruction.

The Statistical Record at Kiowa Agency

Stinchecum provides the most damning statistical evidence in the entire compilation regarding the Declaration of Policy patents:

"In my experience here, during a period of more than six years, I cannot now recall but one case where the Indian has made a success in the handling of his land and that Indian could have made just exactly the same kind of a success had his land been held under trust."

This is an extraordinary admission — one success out of an unspecified but substantial number of patents issued over six years. He then provides precise data on the most recent batch:

"As an illustration, taking the last sixty patents issued as the result of the Declaration of Policy, the records of the various County Seats show that of this number twenty were promptly mortgaged. Of this twenty only one of them has been released. Sixteen tracts have been sold outright. Twenty-four are so far clear, but it is only a question of time until a goodly proportion of these twenty-four tracts will be encumbered. I state this authoritatively, based upon my knowledge of the particular Indians involved and their manner of living."

Breaking this down: - 60 patents issued under the Declaration of Policy - 20 (33%) promptly mortgaged, with only 1 mortgage released - 16 (27%) sold outright - 24 (40%) nominally clear but expected to be encumbered - Total already alienated or encumbered: 36 of 60 (60%), with the superintendent predicting the remainder would follow

This means that within a relatively short period after the Declaration of Policy patents were issued, 60% of Kiowa-area allotments had already been lost or were in jeopardy, and the superintendent expected the figure to approach near-total loss.

Stinchecum's Critique of Blood Quantum as Competency Measure

Stinchecum directly challenged the prevailing policy assumption that blood quantum or educational status could serve as reliable indicators of competency:

"Permit me to say that I believe it is the grossest kind of a mistake to issue patents based on a certain blood status, or educational qualifications as the basis of competency. I have any number of full-blood Indians who cannot speak a word of English whom I would much rather risk with a patent to their land than an even greater number of bright, educated young fellows who have received all the advantages that the Government can give them."

This observation is particularly significant for understanding the Kiowa experience because it suggests that the competency commissions and Declaration of Policy, which used blood quantum and education as primary criteria, were systematically misidentifying who was actually capable of retaining their land. The "bright, educated young fellows" — precisely those the policy was designed to liberate — were the ones most rapidly losing their allotments.

The Proposed Forty-Acre Homestead Reserve

Stinchecum articulated what became one of the most widely endorsed recommendations across the entire survey:

"If I were going to make a suggestion it would be to the effect that the Government in some kind of way make every Indian select forty acres of his original allotment as a homestead and by legislative enactment declare that that forty acres could not be sold or otherwise encumbered or patented to the Indian during the lifetime of the allottee."

He further proposed making such land taxable after the trust period but with taxes chargeable only against other property, not the homestead itself. This would "guarantee to every Indian that as long as he lived he would have forty acres of land upon which to make a home."

The Kiowa Agricultural Context

Stinchecum's observation about Kiowa economic behavior reveals a critical structural problem:

"The great fault with the Indian with whom I am acquainted is that he does not care to crawl before he walks. If he has twenty acres of land under cultivation he feels he must have the same line of heavy, expensive implements that are owned by his white neighbor who might be working ten times as much land."

This suggests that Kiowa allottees, when they did attempt farming, were caught in a trap of over-capitalization relative to their actual cultivated acreage — purchasing expensive equipment for small operations, which created debt that could only be serviced by selling or mortgaging land.

Corroborating Evidence: The Kiowa Agency Farmer's Report

The document also contains testimony from W. D. Brauninger, a farmer who had served three years at the Kiowa Agency "dealing with the Kiowas, Comanches and Apaches" before transferring to the Union Agency at Muskogee (pages 152-153). His testimony provides additional context:

"I could write much more, and cite many concrete illustrations to confirm my statements. But it would all come back to the point set forth above... Handle each individual case strictly on its merits alone and in no other way."

"We have many real old, ignorant, full-bloods that it is far safer to turn loose than many young full-bloods, who possess all the advantages of an education."

Brauninger's experience at Kiowa Agency confirmed Stinchecum's observation that education was a poor predictor of land retention. He also noted the devastating pattern:

"When I take a retrospective view of what we have done and see so many of our young men that we have turned loose without a dollar and without a foot of land, it makes me feel sad. It makes me feel sad to know that, in part, I am responsible for his condition."

This is a rare moment of personal moral reckoning by a federal employee who recognized his own complicity in the dispossession process.

The Broader Oklahoma Context Illuminating Kiowa Experience

J.C. Hart's Overview of Oklahoma Tribes

Superintendent J.C. Hart of the Pawnee Indian Agency provides crucial comparative context. His report covers five tribes (Pawnee, Ponca, Otoe, Kaw, and Tonkawa) and includes a statistical overview showing that approximately three-fifths of the original allotted area was already restricted — meaning two-fifths had already passed out of Indian hands within twenty years:

"These figures may not accurately represent the lands now held by Indians, as there is a considerable acreage that has been patented but is still held by Indians, but indicate that approximately three-fifths of the whole area is now restricted. This reduction has occurred within the past twenty years, and if the same ratio obtains, another twenty years will see the restricted holdings nearly or quite eliminated."

Hart also provides a significant observation about the Kiowa context specifically. He lists his personal experience across multiple agencies:

"Speaking only of the tribes that have come under my immediate observation, ie., the Sioux of South Dakota, in 1893-4, Kiowas in 1894, Arapaho in 1895, Eastern Cherokee in 1896-7, Puyallup Consolidated, 1898-9, Oneidas of Wisconsin, 1900-1919, and Pawnee combined since July, 1919..."

This establishes that Hart had direct experience with the Kiowas as early as 1894, giving him a longitudinal perspective on their trajectory. His general conclusion — that "sound judgment has not always been exercised in granting fee patents, and that in many cases the recipients have not used the privilege wisely" — applied to the Kiowa experience he had witnessed firsthand.

The Cantonment Agency Reports on Cheyenne and Arapaho

The reports from the Cantonment Indian Agency (pages 27-28, 218-221), which administered the Cheyenne and Arapaho tribes adjacent to Kiowa territory, provide important comparative evidence. Farmer William O. Mitchell reported:

"I have worked in the Government service three years with the Cheyenne and Arapahoe Indians and I know of only a few that have kept their land any length of time after they received their patent. Nearly all sell as soon as they can make sale and soon run through with their money."

Superintendent Charles T. Coggeshall's report on the Seger Agency (Colony, Oklahoma) provides even more devastating evidence about the competency commission's impact on nearby tribes:

"During the Spring of 1917 a competency commission visited this reservation and examined a large number of allottees... the result was exceedingly disastrous for each and every Indian who received his patent."

"Out of this total number of 220... each and every allotment has been disposed of, some at ridiculously low prices... Indians disposed of their land immediately upon receipt of the patent (contracts to sell existing, no doubt, prior to the issuance of the patent) and were so eager to procure money that most any price was accepted."

This passage is particularly significant because it reveals that land deals were being arranged before patents were even issued — suggesting a predatory system in which speculators were lining up buyers in advance of the competency commission's visits. Given the geographic and administrative proximity of the Seger, Cantonment, and Kiowa agencies, this pattern almost certainly operated in Kiowa country as well.

McClain Badgly's Testimony on Cheyenne and Arapaho

Lease Clerk McClain Badgly at the Cantonment Agency provides perhaps the most vivid description of the structural conditions affecting western Oklahoma tribes including the Kiowa:

"The majority of these Indians have allotments of 160 acres each, the greater portion of which is leased to white farmers, which is their only income, except their interest in tribal funds... As a rule these people are improvident and shiftless... Only a very few live on or cultivate a portion of their allotments, preferring to live in camps or villages."

"As to patent-in-fee Indians under this agency there is not a single Indian who has obtained a patent to any of his lands and has retained possession of them any length of time. In fact they have not held them any longer than the time required to affect a sale."

Not a single Indian retained their patented land. This is the most absolute statement in the entire compilation and, given the shared geography and administrative culture of western Oklahoma agencies, strongly suggests similar outcomes at the Kiowa Agency.

Badgly also articulated the structural trap:

"The Indian on returning home from school has a much better education and training than the average white child and if he has had the proper economic training instilled into his being as the opportunities have presented he is just as competent to take up life's battles as his white brother... He knows that the Government will look after his interests so he takes his ease."

The Mechanism of Kiowa Land Loss

Drawing together the evidence from the Kiowa Agency and surrounding jurisdictions, the document reveals a multi-stage mechanism of land dispossession:

Stage 1: The Leasing Economy

Before fee patents were issued, Kiowa allottees had developed a dependency on lease income. As Badgly noted, allotments were "leased to white farmers, which is their only income." This created a situation where allottees had no direct relationship with agricultural production on their own land and no experience managing capital.

Stage 2: The Competency Commission and Declaration of Policy

The competency commissions that visited western Oklahoma in 1917 and subsequent years, combined with the Declaration of Policy of 1917 (which mandated patents for Indians of less than one-half blood), created a systematic pipeline for converting trust land to fee simple land. Stinchecum's data shows 60 patents issued under the Declaration of Policy alone at the Kiowa Agency.

Stage 3: Pre-Arranged Sales

As Coggeshall documented at the nearby Seger Agency, "contracts to sell existing, no doubt, prior to the issuance of the patent." Land speculators were positioning themselves to acquire Indian land before the patents were even delivered.

Stage 4: Immediate Alienation

The pattern was remarkably consistent: patents were received, land was sold or mortgaged almost immediately, proceeds were spent on automobiles and consumer goods, and within months the allottee had nothing. Stinchecum's figure of 60% already alienated or encumbered from the most recent batch of 60 patents illustrates this.

Stage 5: Dependency on Relatives

After losing their own land, former allottees fell back on relatives who still held restricted land, creating a cascading burden. As Hart observed:

"It is one of the essentials of Indian hospitality that the visitor must be fed, regardless of the length of his stay... it does tend to discourage individual advancement, financially, and perhaps has more to do with the look of what we call success than we give it credit for."

This cultural practice of mutual support, which had sustained Kiowa communities for generations, became a mechanism through which the economic damage of fee patenting spread beyond the individual allottee to the broader community.

Stage 6: Inherited Land as Final Resource

The document reveals that inherited land became the last economic resource for dispossessed Kiowa allottees. The Five Civilized Tribes reports document how inherited land sales through county probate courts operated with minimal federal oversight and frequently resulted in sales far below market value.

What the AI Extraction Missed

1. The Emotional and Moral Dimension

The AI extraction captured entities and transactions but missed the profound moral anguish expressed by field personnel. Brauninger's statement — "it makes me feel sad to know that, in part, I am responsible for his condition" — represents a crisis of conscience among federal employees who recognized they were administering a system of dispossession. This emotional register is historically significant because it demonstrates that the harm was recognized in real time by the very people implementing the policy.

2. The Predatory Ecosystem

The document describes not just individual transactions but an entire predatory ecosystem surrounding Indian allottees. Stinchecum's reference to "the pleadings of those who for their own reasons wish to be declared competent, the arguments to the Indian Office, to this office and to Congressmen by outside influence" reveals a coordinated system in which speculators, merchants, automobile dealers, and even some attorneys worked together to separate Indians from their land. The AI extracted individual fraud cases but missed this systemic description.

3. The Automobile as Instrument of Dispossession

Across virtually every report in the compilation, the automobile emerges as the primary instrument through which land sale proceeds were dissipated. This was not merely consumer preference but a structural phenomenon: automobile dealers actively targeted new patent holders, and the ongoing costs of automobile ownership (fuel, repairs, replacement) created a continuous drain on resources. At the Coeur d'Alene Agency, Superintendent Lawshe observed: "From an economic standpoint, the automobile is nearly as demoralising to the Indian as whiskey."

4. The Gender Dimension

The document contains significant but underappreciated evidence about gendered patterns of land loss. Superintendent Hutchison at the Shoshone Reservation noted: "Great caution should be exercised in the granting of patents-in-fee to females, as it has been my observation that no matter how competent a woman appears, even though married to a white man, her property is soon squandered by her husband, desertions are frequent, and the woman is usually left with several small children without means of support. The widows and the children made the greatest demands on our ration issue." This pattern almost certainly operated in Kiowa country as well.

5. The Paradox of Competency

Multiple reporters articulated what Superintendent Lipps at Nez Perce called "the incompetency of the competent and the competency of the incompetent" — the observation that older, uneducated full-bloods were often better at retaining land than young, educated mixed-bloods. This paradox directly undermined the theoretical foundations of the fee patent policy and has profound implications for understanding Kiowa land loss, where the policy specifically targeted those deemed most "competent" by blood quantum and educational criteria.

6. Samuel Charger's Indigenous Perspective

The document includes a remarkable letter from Samuel Charger, identified as a Sioux Indian from La Plant, South Dakota (pages 45-49). This is one of the few Indigenous voices in the compilation, and his testimony is devastating:

"My contention is that the Department is going too fast in releasing us Indians from the Federal control... As a rule we Indians are large land owners and some have the best of locations and well improved allotments, once we own lands and have homes, and are in the prime of life to make some progress but today because we are released from Government control we have disposed of our allotments and our homes and some of us are homeless and are in a very sad plight."

"The American Legion says that all ex Indian soldiers should be citizens, and today the majority of our ex-service boys are without lands and are therefore not independent citizens. Such being a very bad example to the tribe."

Charger's observation about returned soldiers losing their land is particularly poignant given that many Kiowa men served in World War I and were subsequently targeted for fee patents under the Declaration of Policy.

Silences and Omissions

1. Kiowa Tribal Response

The document contains no direct testimony from Kiowa tribal members about their experience with fee patents. Samuel Charger's Sioux perspective is included, but no comparable Kiowa voice appears. This silence is itself significant — it suggests either that the Board did not solicit Kiowa testimony or that such testimony was not included in the compilation.

2. Oil and Gas Dimensions

Stinchecum's report makes no mention of oil and gas leasing on Kiowa allotments, despite the fact that southwestern Oklahoma was experiencing significant petroleum development in this period. The omission is striking because oil and gas rights would have significantly affected the value of allotments and the incentives for both allottees and speculators.

3. The Comanche and Apache Experience

Although Stinchecum administered the Kiowa, Comanche, and Apache tribes jointly, his report does not differentiate between them. Brauninger's reference to "Kiowas, Comanches and Apaches" similarly treats them as a unit. This administrative conflation may mask important differences in how each tribe experienced the fee patent process.

4. The Role of Specific Speculators

While the document repeatedly references "grafters," "land men," and "speculators," no specific individuals or companies are named in connection with Kiowa land acquisitions. The Seger Agency report's reference to "contracts to sell existing, no doubt, prior to the issuance of the patent" suggests organized speculation, but the actors remain unnamed.

5. Legal Challenges

The document contains no reference to any legal challenges by Kiowa allottees to fraudulent land transactions, nor to any efforts by the Kiowa tribal government to resist the fee patent policy. This absence may reflect the limited legal options available or may indicate that such efforts occurred outside the scope of this particular survey.

Evidentiary Value

What This Document Proves

  1. That the Superintendent of the Kiowa Indian Agency recognized in 1921 that the fee patent policy was failing catastrophically, with only one success in six years
  2. That 60% of the most recent batch of 60 Declaration of Policy patents at the Kiowa Agency had already been alienated or encumbered
  3. That the superintendent predicted near-total loss of the remaining patents
  4. That blood quantum and educational status were poor predictors of land retention among Kiowa and affiliated allottees
  5. That the pattern of Kiowa land loss was consistent with — and in some cases less severe than — patterns at neighboring Oklahoma agencies where 100% of patented land was lost

What This Document Suggests

  1. That a predatory ecosystem of speculators, automobile dealers, and merchants operated in Kiowa country, pre-arranging land purchases before patents were issued
  2. That the leasing economy had created structural dependency that made allottees vulnerable to rapid land loss upon receiving fee patents
  3. That returned Kiowa soldiers were particularly targeted and vulnerable
  4. That the cultural practice of mutual support among kin networks spread the economic damage of individual land loss across the broader Kiowa community
  5. That federal employees at the Kiowa Agency recognized their complicity in the dispossession process

Questions This Document Raises

  1. What happened to the 24 "clear" patents from Stinchecum's sample of 60? Did they ultimately follow the predicted trajectory?
  2. Who were the specific speculators and land companies operating in Kiowa country?
  3. How did oil and gas development interact with the fee patent process on Kiowa allotments?
  4. What was the total acreage lost by Kiowa allottees through fee patents between 1917 and 1921?
  5. Did any Kiowa allottees or tribal leaders testify before the Board of Indian Commissioners or Congress about their experience?

Research Leads

  1. County seat records at Caddo, Comanche, Kiowa, and Grady counties, Oklahoma, which Stinchecum specifically referenced as the source of his mortgage and sale data for the 60 Declaration of Policy patents
  2. Kiowa Agency correspondence files for 1917-1921, which would contain the individual patent applications, competency commission reports, and Stinchecum's recommendations
  3. Declaration of Policy patent lists for the Kiowa Agency, which would identify the specific 60 allottees and allow tracking of their land through county records
  4. Competency commission records from the 1917 visit to the Kiowa Agency
  5. Board of Indian Commissioners records for any Kiowa testimony that may not have been included in this compilation
  6. The "Investigation Committee for the House Committee of Indian Affairs" referenced by Edward P. Snead, which took testimony on restriction extensions — Kiowa-related testimony may exist in those records
  7. Brauninger's earlier reports from his three years at the Kiowa Agency (approximately 1915-1918), which would provide additional detail on Kiowa conditions
  8. The 1920 annual report referenced by Hart showing land use statistics, which would provide baseline data on Kiowa land retention
  9. Records of the Seger Indian Agency competency commission visit of Spring 1917, which likely operated in conjunction with or immediately before/after the Kiowa Agency visit
  10. Automobile dealer records in Anadarko and surrounding towns, which might document the systematic targeting of new patent holders

Conclusion

This document provides essential evidence that the Kiowa experience with fee patents was one of systematic, rapid, and nearly total land dispossession — a pattern recognized and documented by the very federal officials administering the policy. Superintendent Stinchecum's testimony that he could recall only one success in six years, combined with his statistical demonstration that 60% of the most recent patents had already been alienated, constitutes powerful evidence that the fee patent policy functioned as a mechanism of land transfer from Kiowa allottees to white speculators and settlers. The document further reveals that this outcome was not accidental but was the predictable result of a policy that ignored the structural conditions — the leasing economy, the predatory ecosystem, the cultural practices of mutual support, and the fundamental mismatch between allotment-based individual ownership and Kiowa economic life — that made rapid land loss virtually inevitable upon the removal of federal trust protections.
